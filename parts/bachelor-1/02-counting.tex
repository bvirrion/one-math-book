\chapter{Counting}\label{ch:b1:counting}

Counting \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite sets} sounds elementary --- and quickly becomes subtle.
This chapter defines \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} properly (through bijections, in the
spirit of \cref{ch:b1:logic}), establishes the handful of counting
principles from which everything follows, and derives the classical
counts: lists, \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations}, subsets, \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{binomial coefficients}.

\section{Cardinality of finite sets}

\begin{definition}[Finite set, cardinality]\label{def:b1:counting:card}
For $n \in \N^*$, write $\intint{1}{n} = \{1, 2, \dots,
n\}$. A \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} $E$ is \emph{finite}\index{finite set} when $E = \emptyset$
or there is a bijection from $\intint{1}{n}$ onto $E$ for
some $n \in \N^*$; this $n$ is unique (\cref{thm:b1:counting:welldef})
and is the \emph{cardinality}\index{cardinality} of $E$, written
$\abs{E}$ (with $\abs{\emptyset} = 0$).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Cardinality is well defined]\label{thm:b1:counting:welldef}
If $m \neq n$, there is no bijection from $\intint{1}{m}$
onto $\intint{1}{n}$. More precisely, if $m > n$ there is
no injection from $\intint{1}{m}$ into $\intint{1}{n}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove by induction on $n$ the \omterm{def:b1:logic:statement}{statement}: \emph{for all $m > n$,
there is no injection $\intint{1}{m} \to \intint{1}{n}$}. For $n = 0$ the target is empty and $m \geq 1$: no \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map}
exists at all. Assume the \omterm{def:b1:logic:statement}{statement} for $n$, and suppose $f \colon
\intint{1}{m} \to \intint{1}{n+1}$ is an
injection with $m > n + 1$. If the value $n + 1$ is not attained, $f$
is an injection into $\intint{1}{n}$, contradicting the
induction hypothesis. Otherwise $f(a) = n + 1$ for exactly one $a$;
swap $f(a)$ and $f(m)$ (formally: compose with the transposition of
the two values), so that the new injection $g$ has $g(m) = n + 1$.
Then the restriction of $g$ to $\intint{1}{m-1}$ is an
injection into $\intint{1}{n}$ with $m - 1 > n$ ---
contradiction again.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Pigeonhole principle]\label{cor:b1:counting:pigeonhole}
If $\abs{E} > \abs{F}$, no \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} $f \colon E \to F$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}: some
two elements of $E$ share their image\index{pigeonhole principle}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Write $\abs E = m$, $\abs F = n$ with $m > n$, and choose bijections
$u \colon \intint1m \to E$ and $v \colon F \to \intint1n$. If $f$
were \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}, $v \circ f \circ u$ would be an injection from
$\intint1m$ into $\intint1n$ (a composition of injections,
\cref{prop:b1:logic:comp}), contradicting
\cref{thm:b1:counting:welldef}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interlude: why the swap in the theorem's proof?]\label{rem:b1:counting:swapinterlude}
The proof of \cref{thm:b1:counting:welldef} contains the
chapter's first genuinely clever move, worth replaying slowly.
The obstacle: to apply the induction hypothesis one wants to
delete the last point $m$ of the source \emph{and} the last point
$n+1$ of the target, but $f$ may send some other point $a$ to $n
+ 1$, and then deleting the target point damages the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map}
elsewhere. The cure: compose $f$ with the transposition of the
two \emph{values} $f(a)$ and $f(m)$ --- a bijection of the
target, so injectivity is preserved --- after which the
troublesome value $n + 1$ sits at the harmless position $m$, and
both deletions are clean. This ``normalize first, then cut''
pattern recurs: it is how the derangement recurrence redirects
$\sigma^{-1}(n+1)$ in the weekend problem of this chapter, and
how \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} are patched throughout
\cref{ch:b1:structures}'s problem on the symmetric group.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Injections, surjections and cardinality]\label{prop:b1:counting:injsur}
Let $E, F$ be \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite sets} with $\abs{E} = \abs{F}$, and $f \colon E
\to F$. Then
\[
f \text{ injective} \iff f \text{ surjective} \iff f \text{ bijective}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Suppose $f$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}. Then $f$ is a bijection from $E$ onto $f(E)$,
so $\abs{f(E)} = \abs{E} = \abs{F}$. If $f(E)$ missed a point $y_0$ of
$F$, then $f$ would be an injection of $E$ into $F \setminus \{y_0\}$,
a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} $\abs{F} - 1 < \abs{E}$ --- impossible by the
pigeonhole principle. So $f(E) = F$: $f$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}, hence
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective}.

Suppose $f$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}. Choose for each $y \in F$ one \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{preimage} $s(y)
\in E$; then $f \circ s = \mathrm{id}_F$, so $s$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}
(\cref{prop:b1:logic:comp}). By the previous paragraph applied to $s$
(the cardinalities are equal), $s$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective}. From $f \circ s =
\mathrm{id}_F$ we get $f = \mathrm{id}_F \circ s^{-1} = s^{-1}$, so
$f$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective}. Finally, a \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective} \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} is by definition both
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} and \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}, which closes the cycle of implications.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Finiteness is essential]\label{ex:b1:counting:finiteonly}
On a \emph{\omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite}} \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set}, \cref{prop:b1:counting:injsur} is a powerful
shortcut: any \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} from $E$ to itself is automatically a
\omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation} of $E$ --- half of bijectivity comes for free. Both
implications collapse on infinite \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{sets}: $n \mapsto n + 1$ is
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} from $\N$ to $\N$ but misses $0$, and the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} $\N \to \N$
sending $0 \mapsto 0$ and $n \mapsto n - 1$ for $n \geq 1$ is
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective} but not \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}. Whenever this proposition is invoked,
the finiteness hypothesis is doing real work --- a theme that the
weekend problem of \cref{ch:b1:logic} explores from the other side,
where infinite \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{sets} are precisely those admitting such \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{self-maps}.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Half the work, for free]\label{ex:b1:counting:mod7}
Consider the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} $f$ on $\{0, 1, \dots, 6\}$ sending $k$ to the
remainder of $3k$ upon division by $7$; its table of values is
\[
0,\ 3,\ 6,\ 2,\ 5,\ 1,\ 4 .
\]
Is $f$ a bijection? Injectivity alone suffices
(\cref{prop:b1:counting:injsur}): if $3k$ and $3k'$ have the same
remainder, $7$ divides $3(k - k')$, and since $7$ is prime and
does not divide $3$, it divides $k - k'$ (Euclid's lemma, used at
High School level here and proved in \cref{ch:b1:arith}); with
$\abs{k - k'} \leq 6$ this forces $k = k'$. Surjectivity comes
free --- no need to solve $3k \equiv c$ for each $c$, though the
table confirms every value appears exactly once. The shortcut is
a workhorse: it proves the invertibility of modular
multiplication (\cref{ch:b1:arith}), powers the pairing in
Wilson's theorem, and returns in linear algebra as ``an
endomorphism of a finite-dimensional space is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} iff
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}'' (\cref{ch:b1:findim}).
\end{example}

\section{The counting principles}

\begin{proposition}[Sum and product rules]\label{prop:b1:counting:rules}
Let $E, F$ be \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite sets}.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item If $E \cap F = \emptyset$, then $\abs{E \cup F} = \abs{E} +
        \abs{F}$; more generally, for a \omterm{thm:b1:logic:partition}{partition} of $E$ into pieces
        $E_1, \dots, E_k$, $\abs{E} = \sum_i \abs{E_i}$.
  \item In general, $\abs{E \cup F} = \abs{E} + \abs{F} - \abs{E \cap
        F}$.
  \item $\abs{E \times F} = \abs{E} \times \abs{F}$.
  \item The \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} $F^E$ of all \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} from $E$ to $F$ satisfies
        $\abs{F^E} = \abs{F}^{\abs{E}}$.
  \item $\abs{\mathcal{P}(E)} = 2^{\abs{E}}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(1) Concatenate enumerations: if $E = \{x_1, \dots, x_m\}$ and $F =
\{y_1, \dots, y_n\}$ with no repetition, then $x_1, \dots, x_m, y_1,
\dots, y_n$ enumerates $E \cup F$ without repetition (disjointness).
Induction extends this to $k$ pieces.

(2) $E \cup F$ is the disjoint union of $E$ and $F \setminus E$, and
$F$ is the disjoint union of $F \cap E$ and $F \setminus E$; so
$\abs{E \cup F} = \abs{E} + \abs{F \setminus E} = \abs{E} + \abs{F} -
\abs{E \cap F}$.

(3) $E \times F$ is the disjoint union, over $x \in E$, of the \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{sets}
$\{x\} \times F$, each of \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} $\abs{F}$; apply (1).

(4) A \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} from $E = \{x_1, \dots, x_m\}$ to $F$ is exactly the choice
of the $m$-tuple $(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_m)) \in F^m$; this
correspondence is a bijection, and $\abs{F^m} = \abs{F}^m$ by (3) and
induction.

(5) Subsets of $E$ correspond \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijectively} to \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} $E \to \{0, 1\}$
(send $A$ to its indicator function); apply (4).
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Complement counting]\label{ex:b1:counting:complement}
How many $4$-digit PIN codes (digits $0$--$9$, order matters,
repetition allowed) contain \emph{at least one} repeated digit?
Counting them directly means juggling the cases ``exactly one pair,
two pairs, a triple, a quadruple'' --- five overlapping
configurations. Count the complement instead: all codes number
$10^4 = 10\,000$ (product rule), the codes with four distinct
digits number $10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 = 5\,040$
($4$-arrangements), so the answer is
\[
10^4 - 10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7 = 10\,000 - 5\,040 = 4\,960 .
\]
Nearly half of all PINs repeat a digit. The insight: whenever a
count is phrased with ``at least'' or ``not all'', try the
complement first --- the sum rule guarantees that
$\abs{A} = \abs{E} - \abs{\overline A}$, and the complement is
often a single clean configuration.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Lattice paths]\label{ex:b1:counting:paths}
Count the shortest paths from the corner $(0,0)$ to the corner
$(4, 3)$ of a grid, moving only one step right (R) or one step up
(U) at a time. Every such path takes exactly $7$ steps, of which
$4$ are R and $3$ are U; conversely, any word of length $7$ in the
letters R, U with four R's describes exactly one path. The paths
therefore correspond \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijectively} to the choices of the positions
of the R's:
\[
\binom{7}{4} = 35 .
\]
The insight is the \emph{encoding}: the count became trivial the
moment each path was translated into a word, i.e.\ a subset of
positions --- one more instance of the slogan that a correct count
is a disguised bijection (\cref{met:b1:counting:which}).
\end{example}

\section{Lists, permutations, subsets}

\begin{definition}[Arrangements, permutations, combinations]\label{def:b1:counting:objects}
Let $E$ be a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} with $\abs{E} = n$ and let $0 \leq k \leq n$.
\begin{itemize}
  \item A \emph{$k$-arrangement} of $E$ is an \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} $k$-tuple of
        elements of $E$ (an ordered selection without repetition);
  \item a \emph{permutation}\index{permutation} of $E$ is a bijection
        from $E$ to itself --- equivalently, an $n$-arrangement;
  \item a \emph{$k$-combination} is a subset of $E$ with $k$ elements
        (an unordered selection without repetition). Their number is
        written $\binom{n}{k}$, read ``$n$ choose $k$''
        \index{binomial coefficient}.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The three counts]\label{thm:b1:counting:counts}
With $n = \abs{E}$ and $0 \leq k \leq n$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the number of $k$-arrangements of $E$ is $n (n-1) \cdots
        (n-k+1) = \dfrac{n!}{(n-k)!}$;
  \item the number of \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} of $E$ is $n!$;
  \item $\dbinom{n}{k} = \dfrac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(1) Choose the first coordinate ($n$ ways), then the second ($n - 1$
remaining choices), \dots, then the $k$-th ($n - k + 1$ choices).
Formally, induct on $k$. For $k = 1$ there are $n$ one-term
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} tuples. Assume the count for $k - 1$. Each
$k$-arrangement $(x_1, \dots, x_k)$ is obtained from exactly one
$(k-1)$-arrangement --- its truncation $(x_1, \dots, x_{k-1})$
--- by appending a last coordinate outside $\{x_1, \dots,
x_{k-1}\}$, for which exactly $n - (k - 1)$ values are available.
The $k$-arrangements are thus partitioned, by truncation, into
classes of common size $n - k + 1$ indexed by the
$(k-1)$-arrangements, and the sum rule gives
\[
\frac{n!}{(n-k+1)!}\;(n - k + 1) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} .
\]

(2) is (1) with $k = n$.

(3) Each $k$-subset orders into $k!$ distinct $k$-arrangements, and
every $k$-arrangement arises from exactly one subset: so
$\frac{n!}{(n-k)!} = \binom nk \cdot k!$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Round tables: quotienting by symmetry]\label{ex:b1:counting:roundtable}
In how many ways can $n$ guests sit around a round table, two
seatings being identical when each guest has the same left and
right neighbors --- i.e.\ up to rotation? Each circular seating
corresponds to exactly $n$ linear seatings (cut the circle at any
of the $n$ places), so the $n!$ linear orders collapse in groups
of $n$:
\[
\frac{n!}{n} = (n-1)! \quad\text{circular seatings.}
\]
Equivalently: seat one distinguished guest anywhere (killing the
rotational freedom), then order the remaining $n - 1$ guests
clockwise. For $n = 6$: $120$ tables. The two solutions illustrate
the two standard cures for overcounting: divide by the exact
number of repetitions, or \emph{break the symmetry} by pinning one
object down. Both require the group-of-repetitions size to be the
same for every configuration --- which the proof of the formula
$\binom nk = \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}$ above used as well, with
$k!$ in place of $n$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Adding a constraint]\label{ex:b1:counting:constraint}
Continuing the round table: among the $(n-1)!$ tables of $n \geq
3$ guests, how many seat two given guests $A$ and $B$ \emph{apart}
(not adjacent)? Count the complement. Tables where $A$ and $B$
sit together: glue them into a single block --- $n - 1$ objects
around the table, i.e.\ $(n-2)!$ circular arrangements --- then
order the pair inside its block ($2$ ways): $2\,(n-2)!$ adjacent
tables. Hence
\[
(n-1)! - 2\,(n-2)! = (n-2)!\,\bigl((n - 1) - 2\bigr)
= (n-3)\,(n-2)!
\]
tables keep them apart. Sanity checks: $n = 3$ gives $0$ (around
a triangle, everyone touches everyone) and $n = 4$ gives $2$,
easily listed by hand. The gluing trick --- treat a forced block
as one object, then count its internal arrangements --- is the
standard cure for adjacency constraints, linear or circular.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Basic identities]\label{prop:b1:counting:identities}
For $0 \leq k \leq n$:
\[
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k},
\qquad
\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}
\quad (1 \leq k \leq n-1),
\qquad
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First identity: $A \mapsto E \setminus A$ is a bijection between
$k$-subsets and $(n-k)$-subsets. Pascal's rule\index{Pascal's rule}:
fix an element $a \in E$; the $k$-subsets split into those containing
$a$ (choose the $k - 1$ others: $\binom{n-1}{k-1}$) and those avoiding
$a$ ($\binom{n-1}{k}$). Third identity: both sides count all subsets
of $E$, split by size on the left
(\cref{prop:b1:counting:rules}~(1) and (5)).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Binomial theorem]\label{thm:b1:counting:binomial}
For all $a, b$ in a commutative ring (say $\R$ or $\C$) and $n \in \N$:
\[
(a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^k b^{\,n-k} .
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Expanding $(a+b)(a+b)\cdots(a+b)$ distributively yields one term per
choice, in each factor, of either $a$ or $b$: the term $a^k b^{n-k}$
appears once for each way of choosing which $k$ of the $n$ factors
contribute $a$ --- that is, $\binom nk$ times. (Alternatively: induct
on $n$ using Pascal's rule.)
\end{proof}

\begin{example}\label{ex:b1:counting:binomial}
Two classical specializations: $a = b = 1$ recovers $\sum_k \binom nk
= 2^n$; $a = -1$, $b = 1$ gives $\sum_{k} (-1)^k \binom nk = 0$ for
$n \geq 1$: among the subsets of a nonempty \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set}, exactly half have
even \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality}.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[One identity, two proofs]\label{ex:b1:counting:threen}
The specialization $a = 2$, $b = 1$ of the binomial theorem reads
\[
\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom nk\,2^k = 3^n .
\]
Here is the same identity with no algebra at all. The right side
counts the words of length $n$ over the alphabet $\{0, 1, 2\}$
(product rule). Classify each word by the \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} $K$ of positions
carrying a nonzero letter: choosing $K$ with $\abs K = k$ costs
$\binom nk$, then each position of $K$ independently carries $1$
or $2$: $2^k$ ways. The sum rule over $k$ gives the left side.
Beyond the pleasure of agreement, the two proofs have different
virtues: the algebraic one generalizes to any value of $a$, the
combinatorial one \emph{explains} the formula and adapts to
constraints (forbid the letter $2$ in the last position, say) that
no substitution captures. Keeping both techniques active is the
practical skill this chapter trains.
\end{example}

\begin{method}[Which count applies?]\label{met:b1:counting:which}
Before computing, answer two questions about the selection: does the
\emph{order} matter, and are \emph{repetitions} allowed?
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{l|c|c}
 & order matters & order does not matter \\
\hline
no repetition & $\dfrac{n!}{(n-k)!}$ & $\dbinom{n}{k}$ \\[6pt]
repetition allowed & $n^k$ & (\cref{exo:b1:counting:10}) \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Then hunt for a bijection or a \omterm{thm:b1:logic:partition}{partition} reducing the problem to these
model counts; a correct count is a disguised bijection.
\end{method}

\begin{remark}[Common pitfalls in counting]\label{rem:b1:counting:pitfalls}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Summing non-disjoint cases.} The sum rule requires a
        \omterm{thm:b1:logic:partition}{partition}; if configurations can satisfy two cases at
        once, they are counted twice --- the cure is
        inclusion--exclusion (\cref{thm:b1:counting:inclexcl}) or
        a finer case split.
  \item \emph{Ordered versus unordered.} Choosing ``a committee of
        two'' is $\binom n2$, not $n(n-1)$: decide \emph{before
        computing} whether the selection carries an order, and if
        an ordered count is easier, divide by the number of
        orderings at the end --- but only when every unordered
        object arises from the \emph{same} number of ordered ones.
  \item \emph{Multi-stage choices that are not independent.}
        The product rule needs the number of options at each stage
        to be independent of the previous choices. ``Choose a
        captain, then a different vice-captain'' is fine ($n(n-1)$);
        ``choose two players who get along'' is not a two-stage
        product at all.
  \item \emph{Double-counting by construction.} Building each
        object twice --- e.g.\ counting hands with \emph{at least}
        one ace as (choose an ace) $\times$ (choose $4$ more
        cards) --- overcounts hands with two aces. ``At least''
        almost always calls for the complement
        (\cref{ex:b1:counting:complement}).
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Poker-style count]\label{ex:b1:counting:poker}
From a $52$-card deck, the number of $5$-card hands is
$\binom{52}{5} = 2\,598\,960$. Hands containing exactly one ace:
choose the ace ($4$ ways) then $4$ cards among the $48$ non-aces:
$4 \binom{48}{4} = 778\,320$. The product rule applies because the
choice splits into independent stages.
\end{example}

\begin{method}[Double counting]\label{met:b1:counting:doublecount}
To prove an identity between two counting expressions, find a single
\omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite set} that both sides count --- typically a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of \emph{pairs}
--- and evaluate its \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} in two different orders. The
prototype is the \emph{handshake lemma}: at a party, count the pairs
(person, hand shaken). Summing over people gives $\sum_p d_p$ (each
person $p$'s number of handshakes); summing over handshakes gives
twice the number of handshakes (each involves two people). Hence
$\sum_p d_p$ is even --- so the number of people who shook an odd
number of hands is always even, a nontrivial conclusion obtained
with no formula at all. The same engine runs
\cref{exo:b1:counting:12} and several questions of the weekend
problem below.
\end{method}

\begin{example}[The average subset]\label{ex:b1:counting:averagesubset}
What is the average \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} of a subset of an $n$-element \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set}
$E$, all $2^n$ subsets being equally likely? Double count the
pairs $(A, a)$ with $a \in A$: summing over subsets gives
$\sum_A \abs A$, the total we want; summing over elements gives
$n \cdot 2^{n-1}$ (each of the $n$ elements lies in exactly half
of the subsets --- pair each $A$ containing $a$ with $A
\setminus \{a\}$). Hence
\[
\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{A \subseteq E} \abs A
= \frac{n\,2^{n-1}}{2^n} = \frac n2 :
\]
subsets are, on average, half-full --- as the symmetry $A
\leftrightarrow \overline A$ (which pairs sizes $k$ and $n - k$)
also predicts. Two proofs, one answer, and both avoid the direct
computation $\sum_k k\binom nk$ of \cref{exo:b1:counting:5}: a
well-chosen pairing often replaces an identity.
\end{example}

\section{Inclusion--exclusion}

\begin{theorem}[Inclusion--exclusion]\label{thm:b1:counting:inclexcl}
For \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite sets} $A_1, \dots, A_p$:
\[
\Bigl|\, \bigcup_{i=1}^{p} A_i \,\Bigr|
= \sum_{\emptyset \neq I \subseteq \intint{1}{p}}
(-1)^{\abs{I}+1} \Bigl|\, \bigcap_{i \in I} A_i \,\Bigr| .
\]
For $p = 3$:
$\abs{A \cup B \cup C} = \abs A + \abs B + \abs C - \abs{A \cap B} -
\abs{A \cap C} - \abs{B \cap C} + \abs{A \cap B \cap C}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix an element $x$ of the union and count its contribution to the
right-hand side. Let $J = \{i : x \in A_i\}$, of \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{cardinality} $m \geq
1$. The element $x$ is counted once in $\abs{\bigcap_{i \in I} A_i}$
exactly when $\emptyset \neq I \subseteq J$, with sign
$(-1)^{\abs I + 1}$; its total contribution is
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{m} \binom{m}{k} (-1)^{k+1}
= 1 - \sum_{k=0}^{m} \binom mk (-1)^k = 1 - 0 = 1
\]
by \cref{ex:b1:counting:binomial}. So each element of the union is
counted exactly once.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Counting coprime integers]\label{ex:b1:counting:coprime}
How many integers of $\intint1{120}$ are coprime to $120 = 2^3
\times 3 \times 5$? An integer shares a factor with $120$ exactly
when it is divisible by $2$, $3$ or $5$, so count the complement
of $A_2 \cup A_3 \cup A_5$, where $A_d$ collects the multiples of
$d$. Within $\intint1{120}$, the multiples of $d$ number $120/d$
whenever $d$ divides $120$ --- no floor functions needed --- and
$A_2 \cap A_3 = A_6$, etc. Inclusion--exclusion:
\[
\abs{A_2 \cup A_3 \cup A_5}
= 60 + 40 + 24 - 20 - 12 - 8 + 4 = 88 ,
\]
so $120 - 88 = 32$ integers are coprime to $120$. It is
instructive to regroup the computation as a product:
\[
120 - 88 = 120\Bigl(1 - \frac12\Bigr)\Bigl(1 -
\frac13\Bigr)\Bigl(1 - \frac15\Bigr) = 120 \cdot \frac12 \cdot
\frac23 \cdot \frac45 = 32 :
\]
expanding the three parentheses reproduces exactly the eight
signed terms of inclusion--exclusion, one per subset of $\{2, 3,
5\}$. This product form defines Euler's totient function, whose
arithmetic role appears with \cref{ch:b1:arith}'s congruences and
is developed in the Year 2 volume.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Derangements]\label{ex:b1:counting:derangement}
A \emph{derangement} is a \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation} with no fixed point. Let $A_i$
be the \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} of $\intint{1}{n}$ fixing $i$;
then $\abs{\bigcap_{i \in I} A_i} = (n - \abs I)!$, and
inclusion--exclusion counts the \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} with at least one fixed
point; the derangements number
\[
D_n = n! \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^k}{k!} .
\]
Since $\sum (-1)^k / k! \to \eu^{-1}$ (see \cref{ch:b1:series}), about
$37\%$ of all \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} are derangements, whatever $n$ is.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Where this chapter is used]\label{rem:b1:counting:whereused}
\omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{Binomial coefficients} are the most heavily reused objects of this
chapter: they drive the binomial theorem in \cref{ch:b1:poly}
(expansion of $(X + a)^n$), the Leibniz formula for the $n$-th
derivative of a product in \cref{ch:b1:derivative}, and the
coefficients of Taylor expansions in \cref{ch:b1:taylor}.
\omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{Permutations} return as a group --- with the signature built from
counting inversions --- in \cref{ch:b1:structures}, and the
signature in turn defines determinants in \cref{ch:b1:det}.
Inclusion--exclusion and the counting principles are the \omterm{def:b1:counting:card}{finite}
backbone of discrete probability, developed in the Year 2 volume;
the derangement numbers of \cref{ex:b1:counting:derangement} are
studied in depth in the weekend problem below.
\end{remark}

\section{Exercises}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:1}
A license plate consists of two letters (A--Z), then three digits, then
two letters. How many plates are possible? How many with no repeated
letter among the four?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:2}
How many anagrams (rearrangements of the letters, meaningful or not)
has the word \textsc{orange}? And \textsc{banana}?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:3}
A committee of $4$ people is chosen from $7$ women and $5$ men. How
many committees: in total? with exactly $2$ women? with at least one
man?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:4}
Prove that in any group of $13$ people, two share their birth month;
and that among any $n + 1$ integers chosen from $\intint{1}{2n}$, two are consecutive. \emph{(Pigeonhole both times: name
the boxes.)}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:5}
Compute $\sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k}$. \emph{Hint: differentiate
$(1 + x)^n$, or use $k \binom nk = n \binom{n-1}{k-1}$ (prove it).}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:6}
How many strictly increasing \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} are there from $\intint{1}{k}$ to $\intint{1}{n}$? Deduce the number of
increasing (not necessarily strictly) \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps}. \emph{Hint for the second
count: $f$ increasing $\mapsto$ $g(i) = f(i) + i - 1$.}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:7}
(Vandermonde) Prove, by counting $k$-subsets of a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} split into two
blocks of sizes $m$ and $n$:
\[
\binom{m+n}{k} = \sum_{j=0}^{k} \binom{m}{j} \binom{n}{k-j} .
\]
Deduce $\sum_{j=0}^{n} \binom nj^2 = \binom{2n}{n}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:8}
How many integers in $\intint{1}{1000}$ are divisible by
$2$ or $3$ or $5$? (Inclusion--exclusion; $\lfloor 1000/6 \rfloor$
counts the multiples of $6$, etc.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:9}
Count the surjections from a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of $4$ elements onto a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of $2$
elements; then onto a \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of $3$ elements. \emph{Hint: count the
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{non-surjective} \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} with inclusion--exclusion on the missed values.}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:10}
(Stars and bars) Prove that the number of $k$-selections of $n$
objects \emph{with} repetition, order ignored --- equivalently, the
number of $(x_1, \dots, x_n) \in \N^n$ with $x_1 + \dots + x_n = k$
--- is $\binom{n + k - 1}{k}$. \emph{Hint: encode a solution as a row
of $k$ stars and $n - 1$ bars.}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:11}
Prove the formula of \cref{ex:b1:counting:derangement} for $D_n$ in
detail, and deduce $n! = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} D_{n-k}$ (also
prove this identity directly by classifying \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} by their
fixed-point \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set}).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:counting:12}
For $n \in \N^*$, prove by a double count of pairs (subset, marked
element):
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n\, 2^{n-1},
\qquad\text{then}\qquad
\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 \binom{n}{k} = n(n+1)\, 2^{n-2} .
\]
\emph{For the second: count pairs of marked elements, equal or not.}
\end{exercise}

\section{Problem: Derangements, or the misaddressed letters}

\begin{problem}\label{pb:b1:counting:1}
A secretary puts $n$ letters into $n$ addressed envelopes at random:
what is the chance that \emph{nobody} receives the right letter?
This classical question (Montmort, 1708) leads to the derangement
numbers $D_n$ of \cref{ex:b1:counting:derangement}. The
inclusion--exclusion formula is only the opening move: this problem
develops the recurrences that compute $D_n$, two further independent
proofs of the formula, the striking theorem that $D_n$ is the
integer nearest to $n!/\eu$, the full distribution of fixed points
of a random \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation}, and the curious arithmetic of the sequence
$(D_n)$.\index{derangement}
Throughout, $D_n$ denotes the number of derangements (fixed-point
free \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations}) of $\intint1n$, with the convention $D_0 = 1$
(the empty \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation} has no fixed point).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part I --- Small cases and the fixed-point
census.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute $D_1, D_2, D_3$ directly, and $D_4$ by listing the
        derangements of $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ grouped by the value of
        $\sigma(1)$. (You should find $D_4 = 9$.)
  \item For $0 \leq k \leq n$, show that the number $P_k(n)$ of
        \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} of $\intint1n$ with \emph{exactly} $k$ fixed
        points is $\binom nk D_{n-k}$.
  \item Verify the census for $n = 4$: compute $P_0(4), \dots,
        P_4(4)$ and check that they sum to $4! = 24$. Which is more
        likely for four letters: no match, or exactly one match?
  \item By double counting (\cref{met:b1:counting:doublecount})
        the pairs $(\sigma, i)$ with $\sigma(i) = i$, show that
        \[
        \sum_{\sigma} \abs{\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma)} = n! :
        \]
        on average, a random \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation} has \emph{exactly one}
        fixed point, whatever $n \geq 1$ is.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part II --- Two recurrences and two new proofs of
the formula.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Prove combinatorially, for $n \geq 1$:
        \[
        D_{n+1} = n\,(D_n + D_{n-1}) .
        \]
        (Classify the derangements $\sigma$ of $\intint1{n+1}$ by
        $j = \sigma(n+1)$, then by whether $\sigma(j) = n + 1$; in
        the case $\sigma(j) \neq n+1$, build a bijection with the
        derangements of $\intint1n$ by redirecting the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{preimage} of
        $n + 1$ to $j$.) Check the recurrence numerically up to
        $D_6$.
  \item Setting $u_n = D_n - n D_{n-1}$, deduce from question 5
        that $u_{n+1} = -u_n$, and conclude the second recurrence:
        \[
        D_n = n D_{n-1} + (-1)^n \qquad (n \geq 1).
        \]
  \item From question 6, prove by induction the formula of
        \cref{ex:b1:counting:derangement},
        \[
        D_n = n! \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^k}{k!},
        \]
        --- a proof wholly independent of inclusion--exclusion.
  \item (Binomial inversion) Let $(a_n)$ and $(b_n)$ be two
        sequences such that $a_n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom nk b_k$ for
        all $n$. Prove that
        \[
        b_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^{n-k} \binom nk a_k
        \qquad (n \in \N).
        \]
        (First establish the \emph{trinomial revision}
        $\binom nk \binom kj = \binom nj \binom{n-j}{k-j}$, then
        use the alternating row sum of
        \cref{ex:b1:counting:binomial}.)
  \item Apply question 8 to the identity $n! = \sum_k \binom nk
        D_{n-k}$ of \cref{exo:b1:counting:11} to obtain a
        \emph{third} proof of the formula for $D_n$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part III --- The nearest integer to
$n!/\eu$.} Admit for this part --- the theory is built in
\cref{ch:b1:series} --- that
$\eu^{-1} = \lim_{n \to \infty} s_n$ where $s_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n}
\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}$, with the strict alternating-series bound
$\abs{\eu^{-1} - s_n} < \frac1{(n+1)!}$ for every $n$.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Show that $\bigl| D_n - n!/\eu \bigr| < \frac1{n+1}$ for
        all $n \in \N$.
  \item Deduce the headline theorem: \emph{for every $n \geq 1$,
        $D_n$ is the integer nearest to $n!/\eu$}. Why does the
        argument need $n \geq 1$?
  \item Determine the sign of the error: show that $D_n > n!/\eu$
        exactly when $n$ is even. (Locate the first neglected term
        of the alternating series.)
  \item Compute $D_7$ through $D_{10}$ with the recurrence of
        question 5, then check $D_{10}$ against $10!/\eu$
        ($10! = 3\,628\,800$, $\eu \approx 2.718281828$).
  \item (The hat-check probability) Let $p_n = D_n/n!$ be the
        probability that a uniformly random \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutation} is a
        derangement. Show $\abs{p_n - \eu^{-1}} < \frac1{(n+1)!}$
        and compute $p_6$ to five decimal places. Comment: why is
        the answer to Montmort's question essentially independent
        of $n$ --- already for a dozen letters?
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part IV --- The distribution of fixed points.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Fix $k \in \N$. Show that the proportion of \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations}
        of $\intint1n$ with exactly $k$ fixed points satisfies
        \[
        \frac{P_k(n)}{n!} = \frac{s_{n-k}}{k!}
        \;\xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{}\; \frac{\eu^{-1}}{k!} .
        \]
        (These limiting values, summing to $1$, form the
        \emph{Poisson distribution} of parameter $1$, a central
        object of the probability course in the Year 2 volume.)
  \item By double counting the triples $(\sigma, i, j)$ where $i
        \neq j$ are both fixed by $\sigma$, show that
        $\sum_\sigma \abs{\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma)}\,
        (\abs{\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma)} - 1) = n!$ for $n \geq 2$.
        Combined with question 4: the average of
        $\abs{\mathrm{Fix}}^2$ is $2$, so the ``spread'' (variance)
        of the number of fixed points equals $1$ --- again
        independent of $n$, again matching the Poisson law.
  \item Compute the proportion of \omterm{def:b1:counting:objects}{permutations} having at least one
        fixed point for $n = 4, 5, 6$ (as fractions and to four
        decimal places), and compare with $1 - \eu^{-1} \approx
        0.6321$.
  \item Show directly --- no limits needed --- that $s_{n+2} - s_n
        = (-1)^{n+1}\bigl(\frac1{(n+1)!} - \frac1{(n+2)!}\bigr)$,
        and deduce that the probabilities $p_n = s_n$ of
        question 14 oscillate: $p_0 > p_2 > p_4 > \dots$ and
        $p_1 < p_3 < p_5 < \dots$, the even (resp.\ odd) values
        decreasing (resp.\ increasing) toward the common limit
        $\eu^{-1}$.
  \item (Secret Santa) $n$ people each draw one name from a hat;
        if anyone draws their own name, the \emph{whole} drawing is
        restarted from scratch. Using the standard fact that an
        event of probability $p$ takes on average $1/p$ attempts,
        estimate the average number of complete drawings needed,
        and conclude that the procedure costs about $\eu \approx
        2.72$ drawings on average, essentially independently of
        $n$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part V --- The arithmetic of $D_n$, and a
synthesis.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Refine question 5: show that for fixed $j \in
        \intint2n$, the derangements of $\intint1n$ with
        $\sigma(1) = j$ number exactly $D_{n-1} + D_{n-2}$,
        independently of $j$. Deduce that $n - 1$ divides $D_n$
        for every $n \geq 2$.
  \item Prove that $D_n$ is odd if and only if $n$ is even. (Work
        modulo $2$ in the recurrence of question 6.)
  \item Prove that $D_n \equiv (-1)^n \pmod n$ for $n \geq 1$, and
        check the congruence on the last digit of $D_{10}$.
  \item Show from question 6 that $\dfrac{D_n}{D_{n-1}} = n +
        \dfrac{(-1)^n}{D_{n-1}}$ for $n \geq 3$, so the ratio of
        consecutive derangement numbers is \emph{almost exactly}
        $n$; explain in one sentence why this is consistent with
        $D_n \approx n!/\eu$.
  \item Where exactly did this problem use: (i) the product and
        sum rules; (ii) double counting; (iii) the binomial
        theorem; (iv) the admitted alternating-series bound? One
        sentence each.
  \item Synthesis. The formula for $D_n$ now has three proofs
        (inclusion--exclusion, recurrence plus induction, binomial
        inversion). In a short paragraph, compare what each proof
        \emph{explains}: which one computes fastest, which one
        generalizes to other fixed-point counts, and which one
        reveals why $\eu$ appears in a problem about envelopes.
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}
