\chapter{Linear Maps}\label{ch:b1:linmaps}

The \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} worth studying between \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:def}{vector spaces} are those compatible
with the structure: \emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear maps}}. Their two fundamental
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces} --- \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} and \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} --- measure injectivity and
surjectivity, and in \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite dimension} the rank--nullity theorem
binds their sizes into one conservation law. \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{Projections} and
symmetries, then \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{linear forms} and \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplanes}, close the chapter.

\section{Definitions and first properties}

\begin{definition}\label{def:b1:linmaps:def}
Let $E, F$ be $K$-vector spaces. A \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} $u \colon E \to F$ is
\emph{linear}\index{linear map} when
\[
\forall x, y \in E,\ \forall \lambda \in K, \qquad
u(x + \lambda y) = u(x) + \lambda u(y).
\]
Then $u(0) = 0$ and $u(\sum \lambda_i x_i) = \sum \lambda_i u(x_i)$.
The \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ of linear \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} is itself a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:def}{vector space};
composition of linear \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} is linear, and bilinear in the pair. An
\emph{endomorphism} is a linear $E \to E$; an \emph{isomorphism} is
a \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective} linear \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map} (its inverse is then automatically linear);
$u^{-1}$ of an isomorphism, compositions of isomorphisms, are
isomorphisms.
\end{definition}

\begin{proof}[Proof that the inverse is linear]
Let $u$ be \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear} \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective}, $y, y' \in F$ and $\lambda \in K$.
Put $x = u^{-1}(y)$ and $x' = u^{-1}(y')$. Then
\[
u\bigl(x + \lambda x'\bigr) = u(x) + \lambda u(x')
= y + \lambda y' ,
\]
and applying $u^{-1}$ to both ends: $u^{-1}(y + \lambda y') = x
+ \lambda x' = u^{-1}(y) + \lambda\,u^{-1}(y')$. Nothing about
$u^{-1}$ was computed: linearity transports through the
defining property of $u$ alone --- a pattern worth remembering,
as structure often rides along bijections for free.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[A linear map is known on a basis]\label{prop:b1:linmaps:basis}
Let $(e_1, \dots, e_n)$ be a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} of $E$ and $(v_1, \dots, v_n)$
arbitrary vectors of $F$. There is exactly one \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear map} $u \colon
E \to F$ with $u(e_i) = v_i$ for all $i$. Moreover:
\[
u \text{ injective} \iff (v_i) \text{ free};
\qquad
u \text{ surjective} \iff (v_i) \text{ generates } F .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Existence/uniqueness: every $x$ has unique \omterm{prop:b1:vspaces:coordinates}{coordinates} $x = \sum
\lambda_i e_i$ (\cref{prop:b1:vspaces:coordinates}); linearity
forces $u(x) = \sum \lambda_i v_i$, and this formula does define a
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear map}.

Injectivity: by \cref{prop:b1:linmaps:kernel} below, $u$ is
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} iff its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} is trivial. Now $u\bigl(\sum\lambda_i
e_i\bigr) = 0$ means exactly $\sum\lambda_i v_i = 0$. If $(v_i)$
is \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{free}, this forces every $\lambda_i = 0$, i.e.\ the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}
reduces to $0$: \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}. If $(v_i)$ is linked, a nontrivial
relation $\sum\lambda_i v_i = 0$ produces the nonzero vector
$\sum\lambda_i e_i$ in the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} ($(e_i)$ is \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{free}): not
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}. The two conditions match term for term.

Surjectivity: the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} of $u$ is the \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of all $\sum\lambda_i
v_i$, i.e.\ exactly $\operatorname{Vect}(v_1, \dots, v_n)$, which
equals $F$ iff the family generates.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Kernel and image]\label{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}
For $u \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$:
\[
\ker u = \{x \in E : u(x) = 0\} \subseteq E,
\qquad
\operatorname{im} u = u(E) \subseteq F ,
\]
both \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces} (direct verification with the
criterion).\index{kernel}\index{image}
\end{definition}

\begin{method}[Kernel and image, in practice]
\label{met:b1:linmaps:kerim}
\emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Kernel}}: write $u(x) = 0$ as a system on the \omterm{prop:b1:vspaces:coordinates}{coordinates}
(or coefficients) of $x$, solve, and parametrize --- the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}
comes out with a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} attached
(\cref{met:b1:findim:computedim}). \emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Image}}: it is the \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:span}{span}
of the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images} of \emph{any} generating family of $E$ ---
usually a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}, so $\operatorname{im} u =
\operatorname{Vect}\bigl(u(e_1), \dots, u(e_n)\bigr)$; then
eliminate redundant \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images} to extract a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}. \emph{Shortcut}:
compute whichever of the two is easier and get the other's
dimension for free by rank--nullity
(\cref{thm:b1:linmaps:ranknullity}); when a plausible candidate
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace} for the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} is known, comparing dimensions upgrades
the easy inclusion to equality
(\cref{thm:b1:findim:subspaces}). Both shortcuts are used in
\cref{ex:b1:linmaps:delta} below.
\end{method}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:b1:linmaps:kernel}
$u$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} $\iff$ $\ker u = \{0\}$; $u$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective} $\iff$
$\operatorname{im} u = F$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
As for \omterm{def:b1:structures:group}{groups} (\cref{prop:b1:structures:kernel}): $u(x) = u(y) \iff
u(x - y) = 0 \iff x - y \in \ker u$. The second point is the
definition.
\end{proof}

\section{The rank--nullity theorem}

\begin{definition}\label{def:b1:linmaps:rank}
The \emph{rank}\index{rank!of a linear map} of $u \in \mathcal{L}(E,
F)$ (with $E$ \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}) is $\operatorname{rk} u = \dim
\operatorname{im} u$ --- also the rank of the family
$\bigl(u(e_1), \dots, u(e_n)\bigr)$ for any \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} $(e_i)$ of $E$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Rank--nullity]\label{thm:b1:linmaps:ranknullity}
Let $E$ be \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional} and $u \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$.
Then\index{rank--nullity theorem}
\[
\dim E = \dim \ker u + \operatorname{rk} u .
\]
More precisely, if $S$ is any \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{supplementary} \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace} of $\ker u$ in
$E$, then $u$ restricts to an \emph{isomorphism} from $S$ onto
$\operatorname{im} u$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $S$ satisfy $E = \ker u \oplus S$
(\cref{thm:b1:findim:subspaces}), and let $v \colon S \to
\operatorname{im} u$ be the restriction of $u$.

$v$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}: $\ker v = S \cap \ker u = \{0\}$.

$v$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}: any $u(x)$ with $x = k + s$ ($k \in \ker u$, $s
\in S$) equals $u(s) = v(s)$.

So $v$ is an isomorphism; an isomorphism sends a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} to a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}
(\cref{prop:b1:linmaps:basis}), so $\dim S = \dim\operatorname{im}
u$, and $\dim E = \dim\ker u + \dim S$ concludes.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Building a map to specification]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:tospec}
Construct $u \in \mathcal{L}(\R^3)$ with $\ker u =
\operatorname{Vect}(1,1,1)$ and $\operatorname{im} u = \{z =
0\}$. Sanity first: rank--nullity demands $1 + 2 = 3$ ---
consistent, so a solution may exist. Choose a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} adapted to
the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}, say $\bigl((1,1,1),\ e_1,\ e_2\bigr)$
(\cref{ex:b1:findim:completion}), and prescribe \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images}
(\cref{prop:b1:linmaps:basis}):
\[
u(1,1,1) = 0, \qquad u(e_1) = e_1, \qquad u(e_2) = e_2 .
\]
Then $\ker u \supseteq \operatorname{Vect}(1,1,1)$ and
$\operatorname{im} u = \operatorname{Vect}(e_1, e_2) = \{z =
0\}$; rank--nullity forces $\dim\ker u = 1$, so the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} is
exactly the prescribed line. Explicitly, decomposing $(x, y, z)
= z(1,1,1) + (x - z)e_1 + (y - z)e_2$:
\[
u(x, y, z) = (x - z,\ y - z,\ 0).
\]
The recipe generalizes: a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear map} with prescribed \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} $N$
and \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} $I$ exists exactly when $\dim N + \dim I = \dim E$
--- necessity is rank--nullity, sufficiency is this
construction.
\end{example}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:b1:linmaps:samedim}
If $\dim E = \dim F$ (finite), then for $u \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$:
\[
u \text{ injective} \iff u \text{ surjective} \iff u \text{
bijective}.
\]
In particular this holds for endomorphisms in \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite dimension}. (It
fails in infinite dimension: on $K[X]$, the \omterm{def:b1:derivative:def}{derivative} is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}
but not \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}, and $P \mapsto XP$ is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} but not
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}.)
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{Injective} $\iff \dim\ker u = 0 \iff \operatorname{rk} u = \dim E =
\dim F \iff \operatorname{im} u = F$ (a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace} of full dimension
is everything, \cref{thm:b1:findim:subspaces}) $\iff$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Interpolation, structurally]\label{ex:b1:linmaps:interpolation}
Fix distinct $x_0, \dots, x_n$ and let $u \colon \R_n[X] \to
\R^{n+1}$, $P \mapsto (P(x_0), \dots, P(x_n))$: \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}. Its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}
is $\{P : \deg P \leq n,\ n+1 \text{ roots}\} = \{0\}$
(\cref{cor:b1:poly:nroots}). Equal dimensions $n + 1$: $u$ is an
isomorphism --- existence \emph{and} uniqueness of the Lagrange
interpolant (\cref{thm:b1:poly:lagrange}) in one line.

The same one-line pattern handles data mixing values and
\omterm{def:b1:derivative:def}{derivatives}: $v \colon \R_3[X] \to \R^4$, $P \mapsto \bigl(P(0),
P'(0), P(1), P'(1)\bigr)$ is \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}, and its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} consists of
degree-$\leq 3$ \omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomials} with double roots at $0$ \emph{and}
$1$, i.e.\ divisible by $X^2(X-1)^2$ of degree $4$: only $P =
0$. Equal dimensions again: every quadruple of data $(P(0),
P'(0), P(1), P'(1))$ is realized by exactly one cubic ---
Hermite interpolation, granted by a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} computation before
any formula is written (the weekend problem of \cref{ch:b1:det}
meets its determinant).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Rank--nullity at work: the difference operator]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:delta}
Let $\Delta \colon \R_n[X] \to \R_n[X]$, $P \mapsto P(X+1) -
P(X)$: \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}. \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Kernel}: if $\Delta P = 0$, then $P(0) = P(1) =
P(2) = \dots$, so $P - P(0)$ has infinitely many roots and
vanishes (\cref{cor:b1:poly:nroots}): $\ker\Delta$ is the line of
constants. Rank--nullity: $\operatorname{rk}\Delta = (n + 1) - 1
= n$. Since $\deg \Delta P < \deg P$ for nonconstant $P$ (the top
terms cancel), $\operatorname{im}\Delta \subseteq \R_{n-1}[X]$,
which has dimension exactly $n$: the inclusion is an equality.
Conclusion, with no computation of \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{preimages}: \emph{every}
\omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomial} $Q$ of degree $\leq n - 1$ is a difference $Q = P(X+1)
- P(X)$ --- the discrete antiderivative exists. (Compare the
weekend problem of \cref{ch:b1:vspaces}, where $\Delta$ was
inverted explicitly in the binomial \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}.)
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Rank bookkeeping along a composition]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:rankbookkeeping}
On $\R_2[X]$, compose the \omterm{def:b1:derivative:def}{derivative} $D(P) = P'$ (\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{rank} $2$:
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} $\R_1[X]$, \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} the constants) with itself. Then $D
\circ D = D^2$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} $P \mapsto P''$, with \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} $\R_0[X]$: \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{rank}
$1$. Compare with the general bounds: the crude one gives
$\operatorname{rk} D^2 \leq \min(2, 2) = 2$; the exact formula
of \cref{exo:b1:linmaps:12} accounts for the loss precisely,
\[
\operatorname{rk} D^2 = \operatorname{rk} D -
\dim\bigl(\ker D \cap \operatorname{im} D\bigr) = 2 - 1 = 1 ,
\]
since the constants (\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} of the outer $D$) sit inside
$\R_1[X]$ (\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} of the inner $D$) with dimension $1$. \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{Rank} is
lost exactly where the outer \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} ambushes the inner \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image}
--- the sentence to remember when composition \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{ranks} misbehave.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Kernel and image of the Euler operator]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:euler}
On $\R_n[X]$, let $u(P) = X\,P'$ (\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}: differentiation and
multiplication by $X$ are). \emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Kernel}}: $XP' = 0$ forces $P' =
0$ (a product of \omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomials} vanishes only if one factor does),
so $\ker u$ is the line of constants. \emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Image}}: on the
monomial \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis},
\[
u(X^k) = k\,X^{k} \qquad (k = 0, 1, \dots, n),
\]
so $\operatorname{im} u = \operatorname{Vect}(X, 2X^2, \dots,
nX^n) = \operatorname{Vect}(X, X^2, \dots, X^n)$: the
\omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomials} with zero constant term. Check against rank--nullity:
$\operatorname{rk} u = (n + 1) - 1 = n$, which is indeed the
dimension found. Two remarks worth keeping. First, here
$\operatorname{im} u \oplus \ker u = \R_n[X]$ --- but that is a
\emph{happy accident} of this operator, not a theorem: for the
shift-like $v(P) = P'$ on $\R_1[X]$, $\ker v = \operatorname{im}
v = \R_0[X]$ and the sum is not direct. Second, the relation
$u(X^k) = kX^k$ says each monomial is merely rescaled by $u$ ---
a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} adapted to the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map}, the germ of the eigenvalue idea
developed in the Year~2 volume.
\end{example}

\section{Projections and symmetries}

\begin{definition}\label{def:b1:linmaps:projection}
Let $E = F \oplus G$. The \emph{projection onto $F$ along
$G$}\index{projection} \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} $x = f + g$ (unique decomposition) to
$p(x) = f$; the associated \emph{symmetry}\index{symmetry} is $s(x)
= f - g$. Both are \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}, and $s = 2p - \mathrm{id}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Algebraic characterization]\label{thm:b1:linmaps:projchar}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item An endomorphism $p$ is a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection} (onto some $F$ along
        some $G$) if and only if $p \circ p = p$; then $F =
        \operatorname{im} p = \ker(p - \mathrm{id})$ and $G = \ker
        p$.
  \item An endomorphism $s$ is a symmetry if and only if $s \circ s
        = \mathrm{id}$; then $E = \ker(s - \mathrm{id}) \oplus
        \ker(s + \mathrm{id})$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(1) A \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection} satisfies $p(f + g) = f$ and $p(f) = f$: $p^2 = p$.
Conversely, let $p^2 = p$; set $F = \operatorname{im} p$, $G = \ker
p$. Every $x$ writes $x = p(x) + (x - p(x))$ with $p(x) \in F$ and
$p\bigl(x - p(x)\bigr) = p(x) - p^2(x) = 0$: $E = F + G$. If $y \in
F \cap G$: $y = p(z)$ and $p(y) = 0$, so $y = p(z) = p^2(z) = p(y) =
0$: \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{direct sum}, and $p$ is the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection} onto $F$ along $G$.
Finally on $F$: $y = p(z)$ gives $p(y) = y$, so $F \subseteq
\ker(p - \mathrm{id})$, and conversely $p(y) = y$ puts $y$ in the
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image}.

(2) The correspondence $s = 2p - \mathrm{id}$, $p = \frac{s +
\mathrm{id}}2$ is a bijection between endomorphisms, and under it
\[
s^2 = 4p^2 - 4p + \mathrm{id} = \mathrm{id}
\iff 4p^2 = 4p \iff p^2 = p :
\]
symmetries correspond exactly to \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projections}. Translating the
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces}: $s(x) = x \iff p(x) = x$, so $\ker(s - \mathrm{id}) =
\operatorname{im} p = F$; and $s(x) = -x \iff 2p(x) = 0 \iff x
\in \ker p = G$, so $\ker(s + \mathrm{id}) = G$. The \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{direct sum}
$E = F \oplus G$ of point (1) becomes the announced decomposition
into the fixed vectors and the reversed vectors of $s$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[A projection and its symmetry, explicitly]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:projexplicit}
In $\R^2$, project onto $F = \operatorname{Vect}(1,1)$ along $G =
\operatorname{Vect}(0,1)$. Decompose $(x, y) = a(1,1) + b(0,1)$:
the first coordinate gives $a = x$, the second $b = y - x$.
Hence
\[
p(x, y) = (x, x),
\qquad
s(x, y) = 2p(x,y) - (x,y) = (x,\ 2x - y).
\]
Check the algebra: $p(p(x,y)) = p(x,x) = (x,x)$, and $s(s(x,y)) =
s(x, 2x - y) = (x, 2x - (2x - y)) = (x, y)$. Geometrically, $s$
is the ``oblique reflection'' across the line $y = x$ in the
vertical direction: it fixes $F$ pointwise and reverses $G$. Had
we projected onto the same $F$ along $G' =
\operatorname{Vect}(1,-1)$ instead, the formula would change to
$p'(x,y) = \bigl(\frac{x+y}2, \frac{x+y}2\bigr)$: a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection} is
determined by its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} \emph{and} its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}, never by the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image}
alone.
\end{example}

\section{Linear forms and hyperplanes}

\begin{definition}\label{def:b1:linmaps:forms}
A \emph{linear form}\index{linear form} on $E$ is a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear map}
$\varphi \colon E \to K$. A \emph{hyperplane}\index{hyperplane} of
$E$ ($\dim E = n$) is a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace} of dimension $n - 1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[An evaluation form and its hyperplane]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:evaluationform}
On $\R_2[X]$, the evaluation $\varphi(P) = P(2)$ is a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{linear
form}, nonzero ($\varphi(1) = 1$). Its \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} is the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane}
of \omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomials} vanishing at $2$, i.e.\ (factor theorem,
\cref{thm:b1:poly:factor}) the multiples of $X - 2$ within
$\R_2[X]$:
\[
\ker\varphi = \operatorname{Vect}\bigl(X - 2,\ X(X - 2)\bigr),
\qquad \dim = 2 .
\]
In \omterm{prop:b1:vspaces:coordinates}{coordinates} on $(1, X, X^2)$, $\varphi(a + bX + cX^2) = a +
2b + 4c$: every \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{linear form} on a \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional} space is,
once a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} is fixed, a fixed linear expression in the
\omterm{prop:b1:vspaces:coordinates}{coordinates} --- forms are ``row vectors'', as
\cref{ch:b1:matrices} will make literal, and the coefficient
row here, $(1, 2, 4)$, is a Vandermonde row: evaluation forms
are how the interpolation theory of \cref{ch:b1:det}'s weekend
problem enters linear algebra.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:b1:linmaps:hyperplanes}
The \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplanes} of $E$ are exactly the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernels} of nonzero \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{linear
forms}. Two nonzero forms have the same \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} if and only if they
are proportional.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $\varphi \neq 0$: $\operatorname{rk}\varphi = 1$ (\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} a nonzero
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace} of $K$), so $\dim\ker\varphi = n - 1$: a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane}.
Conversely, let $H$ be a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane}, $(e_1, \dots, e_{n-1})$ a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}
of $H$ completed by $e_n$: the form ``last coordinate'' has \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}
$H$.

Proportional forms share their \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}. Conversely, suppose
$\ker\varphi = \ker\psi = H$ and pick $a \notin H$: every $x$ writes
$x = h + \lambda a$ (as $E = H \oplus Ka$), and
\[
\varphi(x) = \lambda \varphi(a), \qquad \psi(x) = \lambda\psi(a):
\]
so $\varphi = \frac{\varphi(a)}{\psi(a)}\,\psi$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}\label{ex:b1:linmaps:hyperplane}
In $K^n$, a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane} is a solution \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} $\{a_1 x_1 + \dots + a_n
x_n = 0\}$ with not all $a_i$ zero --- the familiar equation of a
plane through the origin in $\R^3$. In function spaces, evaluation
forms $P \mapsto P(1)$ or $f \mapsto \int_0^1 f$ define \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplanes}
of $\R_n[X]$, of $C(\intcc{0}{1})$ (cf.\ \cref{exo:b1:findim:6}).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[One hyperplane, worked three ways]
\label{ex:b1:linmaps:hyperplanerun}
Take $\varphi(x, y, z) = x - 2y + 3z$ on $\R^3$ and $H =
\ker\varphi$. \emph{\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{Basis}}: solve $x = 2y - 3z$:
\[
(2y - 3z,\ y,\ z) = y\,(2, 1, 0) + z\,(-3, 0, 1),
\]
two \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{free} vectors: $\dim H = 2$, a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane}, as
\cref{thm:b1:linmaps:hyperplanes} predicts from $\varphi \neq
0$. \emph{\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{Supplementary} line}: any vector outside $H$ \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:span}{spans} one,
e.g.\ $a = (1, 0, 0)$ ($\varphi(a) = 1 \neq 0$); the
decomposition of an arbitrary $v$ is explicit:
\[
v = \underbrace{\bigl(v - \varphi(v)\,a\bigr)}_{\in\,H}
+ \underbrace{\varphi(v)\,a}_{\in\,\operatorname{Vect}(a)},
\]
since $\varphi\bigl(v - \varphi(v)a\bigr) = \varphi(v) -
\varphi(v)\varphi(a) = 0$. \emph{Proportionality}: if $\psi(x,y,z)
= -2x + 4y - 6z$, then $\psi = -2\varphi$ and both have \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel}
$H$; conversely, any form vanishing on $H$ is a multiple of
$\varphi$ (\cref{exo:b1:linmaps:8}) --- the equation of a
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{hyperplane} is unique up to scale, a fact used constantly for
planes in geometry.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Common pitfalls]
\label{rem:b1:linmaps:pitfalls}
\emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{Kernel} and \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image} live in different spaces}: $\ker u
\subseteq E$, $\operatorname{im} u \subseteq F$; the sum $\ker u
+ \operatorname{im} u$ only makes sense for endomorphisms, and
even then it need not be direct ($u(x, y) = (y, 0)$ has $\ker u
= \operatorname{im} u$; \cref{exo:b1:linmaps:7} characterizes
when directness holds).
\emph{$u^2 = 0$ does not mean $u = 0$}: the same $u(x,y) =
(y, 0)$ squares to zero without vanishing --- what $u^2 = 0$
really says is $\operatorname{im} u \subseteq \ker u$
(\cref{exo:b1:linmaps:5}).
\emph{\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{Injective} $\iff$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective} needs equal \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite
dimensions}}: on $K[X]$, the \omterm{def:b1:derivative:def}{derivative} is \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective} and not
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}, $P \mapsto XP$ \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective} and not \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}
(\cref{cor:b1:linmaps:samedim}); and between spaces of
\emph{different} dimensions, one implication is simply
impossible ($\operatorname{rk} u \leq \min(\dim E, \dim F)$).
\emph{Prescribing \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images} works on a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}, not on any family}:
demanding $u(1, 0) = a$, $u(0, 1) = b$, $u(1, 1) = c$
overdetermines $u$ unless $c = a + b$; a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear map} is \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{free} on a
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}, enslaved everywhere else.
\emph{\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{Rank} is not preserved by composition}: it can only drop,
$\operatorname{rk}(vu) \leq \min(\operatorname{rk} u,
\operatorname{rk} v)$ (\cref{exo:b1:linmaps:4}), with exact loss
measured in \cref{exo:b1:linmaps:12}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Where these maps go]
\label{rem:b1:linmaps:whereused}
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{Linear maps} are about to become \emph{matrices}: once bases are
fixed, \cref{ch:b1:matrices} encodes every $u \in \mathcal{L}(E,
F)$ by a rectangular array, and composition becomes the matrix
product --- rank--nullity then drives the theory of \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}
systems in \cref{ch:b1:det}. \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{Projections} return in
\cref{ch:b1:euclid} in their most useful special case, the
\emph{orthogonal} \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection}, where the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} is chosen
perpendicular to the \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image}. The weekend problem below pushes the
projector algebra as far as first-year tools reach, up to
Fitting's lemma; the Year~2 volume goes further with the trace
and with eigenvalue theory, for which projectors onto stable
\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces} are the basic building blocks.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Perspectives inside Book 3: rank--nullity three more times]
\label{rem:b1:linmaps:perspectives}
The conservation law $\dim E = \dim\ker u + \operatorname{rk} u$
will be re-read three times before the volume ends. In
\cref{ch:b1:det} it becomes the shape of solution \omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{sets}: a
compatible system with $p$ unknowns and \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{rank} $r$ has a solution
\omterm{def:b1:logic:sets}{set} of dimension $p - r$ --- \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernel} dimension in disguise. In
\cref{ch:b1:euclid} it splits orthogonally, $\dim F + \dim
F^\perp = \dim E$, and powers every distance computation. In
the weekend problem of \cref{ch:b1:multivar}, it is the
accountant of least squares: $n$ observations, $2$ fitted
parameters, $n - 2$ residual dimensions, and the Pythagorean
identity $\norm b^2 = \norm p^2 + \norm{b - p}^2$ is
rank--nullity's Euclidean shadow. One theorem, four costumes.
\end{remark}

\section{Exercises}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:1}
Which \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{maps} are \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:def}{linear}?
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $\R^2 \to \R^2$, $(x, y) \mapsto (x + y, x - 2y)$;
  \item $\R^2 \to \R$, $(x, y) \mapsto xy$;
  \item $\R[X] \to \R[X]$, $P \mapsto P' + XP$;
  \item $\mathcal{F}(\R,\R) \to \R$, $f \mapsto f(3)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:2}
Let $u \colon \R^3 \to \R^3$, $(x,y,z) \mapsto (x + y - z,\; 2x + y
+ z,\; 3x + 2y)$. Determine $\ker u$ (\omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis}, dimension),
$\operatorname{rk} u$, and a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} of $\operatorname{im} u$. Is $u$
\omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{injective}? \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{surjective}?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:3}
Let $u \colon \R_n[X] \to \R_n[X]$, $P \mapsto P - P'$. Prove that
$u$ is an isomorphism: once via $\ker u$, once by exhibiting the
inverse \emph{(consider $P + P' + P'' + \dots$)}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:4}
Let $u \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$ and $v \in \mathcal{L}(F, G)$, spaces
\omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}. Prove:
\[
\operatorname{rk}(v \circ u) \leq
\min\bigl(\operatorname{rk} u,\ \operatorname{rk} v\bigr).
\]
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:5}
Let $u$ be an endomorphism of $E$ (\omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}) with $u^2 =
0$. Prove that $\operatorname{im} u \subseteq \ker u$, hence
$\operatorname{rk} u \leq \frac{\dim E}{2}$. For $E = \R^2$, give an
example with equality.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:6}
Let $p, q$ be \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projections} of $E$ with $p \circ q = q \circ p$.
Prove that $p \circ q$ is a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projection}, with
\[
\operatorname{im}(pq) = \operatorname{im} p \cap \operatorname{im}
q ,
\qquad
\ker (pq) = \ker p + \ker q .
\]
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:7}
Let $u \in \mathcal{L}(E)$, $E$ \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}. Prove the
equivalence of:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $E = \ker u \oplus \operatorname{im} u$;
  \item $\ker u = \ker u^2$;
  \item $\operatorname{im} u = \operatorname{im} u^2$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:8}
Let $\varphi, \psi$ be \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:forms}{linear forms} on $E$ with $\ker\varphi
\subseteq \ker\psi$. Prove $\psi = \lambda\varphi$ for some
$\lambda \in K$ (including the degenerate cases).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:9}
Let $u \in \mathcal{L}(E)$ with $\dim E = n$, and suppose $u^n = 0$
but $u^{n-1} \neq 0$ (a \emph{maximally nilpotent} endomorphism).
Pick $x$ with $u^{n-1}(x) \neq 0$; prove that $\bigl(x, u(x), \dots,
u^{n-1}(x)\bigr)$ is a \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{basis} of $E$. \emph{(Apply powers of $u$ to a
null combination, starting with $u^{n-1}$.)}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:10}
Let $f \in \mathcal{L}(\R^n)$ with $f \circ f = -\mathrm{id}$.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Prove that $f$ is an isomorphism and that no $x \neq 0$
        satisfies $f(x) = \lambda x$ with $\lambda \in \R$.
  \item Prove that $n$ is even. \emph{Hint: pick $x_1 \neq 0$; show
        $\operatorname{Vect}(x_1, f(x_1))$ is a plane stable under
        $f$; choose $x_2$ outside it and iterate, proving that
        $\bigl(x_1, f(x_1), x_2, f(x_2), \dots\bigr)$ stays \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:free}{free}.}
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:11}
Let $u, v \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$, spaces \omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}.
Prove the two-sided bound
\[
\abs{\operatorname{rk} u - \operatorname{rk} v}
\;\leq\; \operatorname{rk}(u + v)
\;\leq\; \operatorname{rk} u + \operatorname{rk} v .
\]
\emph{(For the \omterm{def:b1:reals:bounds}{upper bound}, compare $\operatorname{im}(u+v)$ with
$\operatorname{im} u + \operatorname{im} v$; for the lower one,
apply the \omterm{def:b1:reals:bounds}{upper bound} cleverly.)}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:b1:linmaps:12}
(Frobenius' inequality) Let $u \in \mathcal{L}(E, F)$, $w \in
\mathcal{L}(F, G)$ and $v \in \mathcal{L}(G, H)$, all spaces
\omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional}. Prove the exact formula
\[
\operatorname{rk}(v \circ w) = \operatorname{rk} w -
\dim\bigl(\ker v \cap \operatorname{im} w\bigr),
\]
and deduce Frobenius' inequality
\[
\operatorname{rk}(v \circ w) + \operatorname{rk}(w \circ u)
\;\leq\; \operatorname{rk} w + \operatorname{rk}(v \circ w \circ
u) .
\]
Check that the case $w = \mathrm{id}_F$ is Sylvester's
inequality, proved in matrix form in \cref{exo:b1:matrices:10}.
\end{exercise}

\section{Problem: projector calculus and Fitting's lemma}

\begin{problem}\label{pb:b1:linmaps:1}
\omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{Projections} are the endomorphisms that \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{direct sums} produce, and
conversely: every identity $E = F_1 \oplus \dots \oplus F_k$ is
secretly a family of projectors summing to the identity. This
problem develops that dictionary --- the algebra of one
projector, of two, of $k$ --- then applies the same
stabilization ideas to an arbitrary endomorphism and proves
\emph{Fitting's lemma}: every endomorphism of a
\omterm{def:b1:findim:def}{finite-dimensional} space splits into a nilpotent part and an
invertible part. Throughout, $E$ is a $K$-vector space of
dimension $n$, and \emph{projector} means $p \in \mathcal{L}(E)$
with $p^2 = p$ (\cref{thm:b1:linmaps:projchar}).
\index{projection}\index{Fitting's lemma}\index{nilpotent}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part I --- The algebra around one projector.}
Let $p$ be a projector, $p \neq 0$, $p \neq \mathrm{id}$.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Show that $\mathrm{id} - p$ is a projector and identify
        $\operatorname{im}(\mathrm{id} - p)$ and
        $\ker(\mathrm{id} - p)$.
  \item Compute $(\lambda\,\mathrm{id} + \mu\,p)^2$ and determine
        all pairs $(\lambda, \mu) \in K^2$ for which
        $\lambda\,\mathrm{id} + \mu\,p$ is a projector.
  \item Show that the plane $\operatorname{Vect}(\mathrm{id}, p)$
        of $\mathcal{L}(E)$ is stable under composition, and that
        for every \omterm{def:b1:poly:def}{polynomial} $Q \in K[X]$,
        \[
        Q(p) = Q(0)\,\mathrm{id} + \bigl(Q(1) -
        Q(0)\bigr)\,p .
        \]
  \item Determine for which $(\lambda, \mu)$ the \omterm{def:b1:logic:map}{map}
        $\lambda\,\mathrm{id} + \mu\,p$ is invertible, and give
        its inverse in the form $\alpha\,\mathrm{id} + \beta\,p$.
        Interpret the answer through the action of
        $\lambda\,\mathrm{id} + \mu\,p$ on $\operatorname{im} p$
        and on $\ker p$.
  \item Let $p'$ be another projector with the \emph{same \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{image}}
        $\operatorname{im} p' = \operatorname{im} p$. Show that
        $p\,p' = p'$ and $p'\,p = p$. What do these identities
        say about composing \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:projection}{projections} onto the same \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspace}
        along different \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernels}?
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part II --- Two projectors.} Let $p, q$ be
projectors of $E$; assume the characteristic is not $2$ (true
for $K = \R, \C$).
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Suppose $p + q$ is a projector. Expanding $(p + q)^2$,
        show $pq + qp = 0$; composing with $p$ on the left, then
        on the right, deduce $pq = qp$, and conclude $pq = qp =
        0$.
  \item Conversely, suppose $pq = qp = 0$. Show that $p + q$ is a
        projector, with
        \[
        \operatorname{im}(p + q) = \operatorname{im} p \oplus
        \operatorname{im} q,
        \qquad
        \ker(p + q) = \ker p \cap \ker q .
        \]
  \item Show that $p - q$ is a projector if and only if $pq = qp
        = q$. \emph{(Apply questions 6--7 to $\mathrm{id} - p$
        and $q$.)}
  \item Show the geometric meaning of $pq = qp = q$: it holds if
        and only if $\operatorname{im} q \subseteq
        \operatorname{im} p$ and $\ker p \subseteq \ker q$. (One
        then writes $q \leq p$: ``$q$ projects onto less, along
        more''.)
  \item Now let $p, q$ commute. Recall from
        \cref{exo:b1:linmaps:6} that $pq$ is the projector onto
        $\operatorname{im} p \cap \operatorname{im} q$ along
        $\ker p + \ker q$. Show that $r = p + q - pq$ is a
        projector with
        \[
        \operatorname{im} r = \operatorname{im} p +
        \operatorname{im} q,
        \qquad
        \ker r = \ker p \cap \ker q .
        \]
        \emph{(Consider $\mathrm{id} - r = (\mathrm{id} -
        p)(\mathrm{id} - q)$.)}
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part III --- Decompositions of the identity.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Let $E = F_1 \oplus \dots \oplus F_k$ and, for $x = x_1 +
        \dots + x_k$ (unique decomposition, $x_i \in F_i$), set
        $p_i(x) = x_i$. Show that each $p_i$ is a projector, that
        $p_i p_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$, and that $p_1 + \dots + p_k
        = \mathrm{id}$; identify $\operatorname{im} p_i$ and
        $\ker p_i$.
  \item Conversely, let $p_1, \dots, p_k \in \mathcal{L}(E)$
        satisfy $p_1 + \dots + p_k = \mathrm{id}$ and $p_i p_j =
        0$ for all $i \neq j$. Show that each $p_i$ is a
        projector and that $E = \operatorname{im} p_1 \oplus
        \dots \oplus \operatorname{im} p_k$.
  \item Two projectors with $p + q = \mathrm{id}$: show that $pq
        = qp = 0$ holds automatically.
  \item Three projectors with $p + q + r = \mathrm{id}$: show
        that $p + q$ is a projector, and deduce from question 6
        that \emph{all} pairwise products vanish --- hence $E =
        \operatorname{im} p \oplus \operatorname{im} q \oplus
        \operatorname{im} r$, with no hypothesis on the products.
  \item For $k$ projectors with $p_1 + \dots + p_k =
        \mathrm{id}$: show first that for any \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces}, $\dim
        (F_1 + \dots + F_k) \leq \dim F_1 + \dots + \dim F_k$,
        with equality if and only if the sum is direct; then
        show $E = \operatorname{im} p_1 + \dots +
        \operatorname{im} p_k$, and prove that \emph{if}
        moreover $\sum_i \operatorname{rk} p_i \leq n$, the sum
        is direct and $p_i p_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part IV --- Iterated \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{kernels}: Fitting's lemma.}
Let $u \in \mathcal{L}(E)$, $\dim E = n$.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Show the two chains, valid for every $k \geq 0$:
        \[
        \ker u^k \subseteq \ker u^{k+1},
        \qquad
        \operatorname{im} u^{k+1} \subseteq \operatorname{im}
        u^k .
        \]
  \item Show that if $\ker u^{r} = \ker u^{r+1}$ for some $r$,
        then $\ker u^{k} = \ker u^{r}$ for all $k \geq r$; state
        and prove the analogous stabilization for \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images}.
  \item Deduce that there is a smallest integer $r$ with $\ker
        u^{r} = \ker u^{r+1}$, that $r \leq n$, and that \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:kerim}{images}
        stabilize at the same $r$.
  \item (Fitting's lemma) Prove that
        \[
        E \;=\; \ker u^{r} \,\oplus\, \operatorname{im} u^{r} .
        \]
  \item Show that both \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces} are stable under $u$, that the
        restriction of $u$ to $\ker u^{r}$ is nilpotent, and that
        the restriction of $u$ to $\operatorname{im} u^{r}$ is an
        isomorphism of $\operatorname{im} u^{r}$: every
        endomorphism is, on a canonical \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{direct sum}, ``nilpotent
        plus invertible''.
  \item Let $\pi$ be the projector onto $\ker u^{r}$ along
        $\operatorname{im} u^{r}$. Show that $\pi \circ u = u
        \circ \pi$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part V --- A worked case, and synthesis.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Let $u(x, y, z) = (y, 0, z)$ on $\R^3$. Compute $u^2$ and
        $u^3$, determine the stabilization index $r$, the
        \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:subspace}{subspaces} $\ker u^{r}$ and $\operatorname{im} u^{r}$, the
        Fitting projector $\pi$, and verify on the formulas that
        $\pi u = u\pi$ and that $u$ is nilpotent on one factor,
        \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective} on the other.
  \item Show the equivalences: $u$ nilpotent $\iff$ $\ker u^{r} =
        E$ $\iff$ $\pi = \mathrm{id}$; and deduce that a
        nilpotent endomorphism of an $n$-dimensional space
        always satisfies $u^{n} = 0$ (the nilpotency index never
        exceeds the dimension).
  \item (Uniqueness) Suppose $E = A \oplus B$ with $A, B$ stable
        under $u$, the restriction $u|_A$ nilpotent and $u|_B$
        \omterm{def:b1:logic:inj}{bijective}. Prove $A = \ker u^{r}$ and $B =
        \operatorname{im} u^{r}$: the Fitting decomposition is
        unique.
  \item Synthesis, in four sentences: what dictionary Part III
        establishes between \omterm{def:b1:vspaces:sum}{direct sums} and families of
        projectors; why question 14 needed no product
        hypotheses while question 15 needed a \omterm{def:b1:linmaps:rank}{rank} hypothesis
        (and which Year~2 tool, the trace, removes it); in what
        sense Fitting's lemma is the stabilized version of
        \cref{exo:b1:linmaps:7}; and what the two factors of
        Fitting become in the eigenvalue theory of the Year~2
        volume. Name the theorem proved in Part IV.
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}
