\section*{Chapter \ref{ch:g3:shapes} --- Shapes and Right Angles}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:1}
Typical right angles: the corners of the board, of a sheet of paper,
of the door. A non-right angle: the opening of a pair of scissors,
the hands of the clock at 1 o'clock. Each is checked with the set
square (\cref{met:g3:shapes:check}) --- never just by eye.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:2}
Construction: one side of $6$ cm, right angles at both ends with the
set square, sides of $4$ cm, close the shape. Marks: $4$ little
squares in the corners, one tick on each $6$ cm side, a double tick
on each $4$ cm side.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:3}
A square has $4$ right angles and $4$ equal sides --- all marked.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:4}
Grid constructions; the grid guarantees the right angles, and
counting squares guarantees the lengths.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:5}
``Every square is a rectangle'': \emph{true} --- it has $4$ right
angles, which is all a rectangle asks.

``Every rectangle is a square'': \emph{false} --- a $6 \times 4$
rectangle has unequal sides.

``A triangle can have two right angles'': \emph{false} --- the two
sides drawn from the two right angles would be perpendicular to the
same base line, hence never meet to close the triangle.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:6}
$OA = 4$ cm (the radius). $OB = 4$ cm too: \emph{every} point of
the circle is at the same distance from the center.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:7}
The rectangle is cut into three triangles: two right triangles
($AMD$ and $MBC$, right-angled at $A$ and $B$) and the middle
triangle $MCD$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:8}
With at least one right angle: the square, the rectangle, the right
triangle, and the letter L (its inner and outer corners). The circle
has no corner at all.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:9}
Each side crosses ``$2$ right, $1$ up'' (or the turned versions
``$1$ right, $2$ down'', etc.): every side is the diagonal of a
$2 \times 1$ block of squares, so all four have the same length.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:10}
One correct program: ``1. Draw a rectangle $ABCD$ with $AB = 3$ cm
(bottom) and $BC = 2$ cm. 2. On top of the side $[DC]$, draw the
square $DCEF$ of side $3$ cm, outside the rectangle.'' Any clear,
numbered, complete program is correct --- test it by following it
word for word.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g3:shapes:11}
$1 \times 1$ squares: $9$. $2 \times 2$ squares: $4$.
$3 \times 3$: $1$. Total: $9 + 4 + 1 = 14$ squares.
\end{solution}
