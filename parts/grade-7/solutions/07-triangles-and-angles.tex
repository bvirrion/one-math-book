\begin{solution}{exo:g7:triangles:4}
Apex $40^\circ$: the base angles share $180 - 40 = 140^\circ$
equally: $70^\circ$ each.

Base angle $70^\circ$: the two base angles are equal, so the apex is
$180 - 2 \times 70 = 40^\circ$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:triangles:5}
$(6, 8, 12)$: $12 < 6 + 8 = 14$: triangle exists.

$(5, 5, 11)$: $11 > 5 + 5 = 10$: impossible.

$(4, 9, 13)$: $13 = 4 + 9$: flat ``triangle'' --- the points are
aligned, no genuine triangle.

$(7, 7, 7)$: $7 < 14$: \omterm{def:g6:shapes:triangles}{equilateral triangle}.
\end{solution}

\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:triangles:9}
Let $c$ be the third side. The triangle inequality demands
$c < 8 + 3 = 11$ and also $8 < c + 3$, i.e.\ $c > 5$. So
$5 < c < 11$: the length $7$ cm works; the length $12$ cm (or $4$ cm)
does not.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:triangles:10}
$\widehat A + \widehat B = 180 - 90 = 90^\circ$, and
$\widehat A = 2\widehat B$, so $3\widehat B = 90^\circ$:
$\widehat B = 30^\circ$ and $\widehat A = 60^\circ$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:triangles:11}
The diagonal $[AC]$ splits $ABCD$ into triangles $ABC$ and $ACD$.
The four angles of the \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{quadrilateral} are exactly the six angles of
the two triangles, regrouped (the angles at $A$ and at $C$ are each
split in two). Total: $180 + 180 = 360^\circ$. A \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{pentagon} splits from
one \omterm{def:g5:solids:def}{vertex} into three triangles: $3 \times 180 = 540^\circ$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:g7:triangles:1}
\textbf{1.} The diagonals from one \omterm{def:g5:solids:def}{vertex} of a \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon} cut it
into $4$ triangles, so the interior angles total
$4 \times 180 = 720^\circ$.

\textbf{2.} From one \omterm{def:g5:solids:def}{vertex}, diagonals go to all \omterm{def:g5:solids:def}{vertices} except
itself and its two neighbours: $n - 3$ diagonals, cutting the
\omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{polygon} into $n - 2$ triangles. Every interior angle of the
\omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{polygon} is distributed among the triangles, so the \omterm{def:g1:addition:def}{sum} is
\[
(n - 2) \times 180^\circ .
\]
Check: $n = 3$: $180^\circ$; $n = 4$: $360^\circ$
(\cref{exo:g7:triangles:11}); $n = 5$: $540^\circ$; $n = 6$:
$720^\circ$.

\textbf{3.} Decagon: $8 \times 180 = 1\,440^\circ$. Dodecagon:
$10 \times 180 = 1\,800^\circ$.

\textbf{4.} Dividing by $n$: triangle $60^\circ$; square
$90^\circ$; \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{pentagon} $\frac{540}{5} = 108^\circ$; \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon}
$\frac{720}{6} = 120^\circ$; \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{octagon} $\frac{1080}{8} =
135^\circ$.

\textbf{5.} Adding a \omterm{def:g5:solids:def}{vertex} adds one more triangle to the fan:
the new \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{polygon} needs $n - 1$ triangles where the old needed
$n - 2$. One extra triangle, one extra $180^\circ$.

\textbf{6.} The walker turns by the supplement:
$180^\circ - \widehat A$. For the $40$--$60$--$80$ triangle, the
turns are $140^\circ$, $120^\circ$ and $100^\circ$.

\textbf{7.} $140 + 120 + 100 = 360^\circ$: one full turn.

\textbf{8.} Over the whole walk, your nose starts and ends
pointing the same way, having swept around exactly once: all the
turning done at the corners amounts to one complete revolution,
$360^\circ$ --- whatever the number of corners and the shape of
the block. (The fact is beautifully independent of $n$.)

\textbf{9.} At each corner, interior $+$ turn $= 180^\circ$.
Summing over the $n$ corners:
\[
\text{(sum of interiors)} + 360^\circ = 180^\circ \times n,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\text{sum of interiors} = (n - 2) \times 180^\circ
\]
--- the formula of question 2, re-proved by walking.

\textbf{10.} Interior $150^\circ$ means each turn is
$30^\circ$, and $n = \frac{360}{30} = 12$: the regular dodecagon.
Interior $155^\circ$ would mean turns of $25^\circ$, and
$\frac{360}{25} = 14.4$ is not a whole number of corners: no
such regular \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{polygon} exists.

\textbf{11.} Triangle ($60^\circ$): $6$ meet,
$6 \times 60 = 360$. Square ($90^\circ$): $4$ meet. \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{Hexagon}
($120^\circ$): $3$ meet, $3 \times 120 = 360$. All three close
up perfectly.

\textbf{12.} Three \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{pentagon} corners: $3 \times 108 = 324^\circ$
--- a gap of $36^\circ$ remains. Four corners:
$4 \times 108 = 432^\circ > 360^\circ$ --- overlap. Neither
works: regular \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{pentagons} cannot tile the plane.

\textbf{13.} At a tiling corner at least $3$ tiles meet. A
regular \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{polygon} with more than six sides has interior angles
\emph{greater} than $120^\circ$ (question 4 and the growth of
the angle with $n$), so three corners exceed
$3 \times 120 = 360^\circ$: overlap, impossible. With six sides
exactly, $3 \times 120 = 360$ works; below six, questions 11
and 12 sort the cases. The complete list of regular tilers:
triangle, square, \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon}.

\textbf{14.} \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{Octagons} and squares:
$135 + 135 + 90 = 360^\circ$: the corner closes --- the classic
street pattern. Square, \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon}, dodecagon: the dodecagon's
turn is $\frac{360}{12} = 30^\circ$, so its interior angle is
$150^\circ$, and $90 + 120 + 150 = 360^\circ$: it works too.

\textbf{15.} Honeycomb corners: $3 \times 120 = 360^\circ$,
perfect. Among triangle, square and \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon} tiles of equal
\omterm{def:g6:measure:area}{area}, the \omterm{def:g4:geometry:polygon}{hexagon} has the shortest boundary --- it is the
closest of the three to Dido's circle
(\cref{pb:g6:measure:1}) --- so hexagonal cells enclose the
same honey with the least wax: the bees tile like geometers.
\end{solution}
