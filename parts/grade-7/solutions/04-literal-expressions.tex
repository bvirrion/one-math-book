\begin{solution}{exo:g7:literal:8}
$3(x+4) = 3x + 12$: \emph{true} for every $x$ (distributivity ---
this is a proof, no test needed).

$2x + 5 = 7x$: test $x = 2$: left $9$, right $14$ --- \emph{false} in
general (it holds only for $x = 1$).

$x + x = x^2$: test $x = 3$: left $6$, right $9$ --- \emph{false} in
general ($x + x = 2x$).
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:literal:9}
\emph{1.} Length: $w + 3$. \omterm{def:g6:measure:perimeter}{Perimeter}:
\[
P = 2\bigl(w + (w + 3)\bigr) = 2(2w + 3) = 4w + 6 .
\]

\emph{2.} For $w = 5$: directly, the sides are $5$ and $8$, so
$P = 2 \times 13 = 26$ cm; with the formula, $4 \times 5 + 6 = 26$ cm.
They agree.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:literal:10}
Following the steps with the letter $n$:
\[
n \ \to\ n + 6 \ \to\ 3(n + 6) = 3n + 18 \ \to\ 3n + 18 - 18 = 3n
\ \to\ \frac{3n}{n} = 3 .
\]
Everyone gets $3$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g7:literal:11}
As a \omterm{ex:g1:subtraction:difference}{difference}: $A = a^2 - b^2$. By cutting the L into two
rectangles: a full-width strip of height $a - b$ and, on top of it, a
narrower rectangle:
\[
A = a(a - b) + b(a - b)
\]
(bottom rectangle $a$ wide and $a - b$ tall, top rectangle $a - b$
wide and $b$ tall gives the variant $a(a-b) + (a-b)b$ --- the same).
For $a = 5$, $b = 2$: $a^2 - b^2 = 25 - 4 = 21$, and
$5 \times 3 + 2 \times 3 = 15 + 6 = 21$. Both expressions agree ---
they always do: this is the identity $a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b)$ of
\cref{ch:g9:algebra} in disguise.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:g7:literal:1}
\textbf{1.} Figures $1$ to $4$ need $4$, $7$, $10$, $13$
matches.

\textbf{2.} A new square shares one side with the previous
figure, so only $3$ of its $4$ sides are new matches. From
figure $4$ ($13$ matches), six more squares cost
$6 \times 3 = 18$: figure $10$ needs $13 + 18 = 31$ matches.

\textbf{3.} Reading the row from the left: it starts with one
vertical match, and each of the $n$ squares then contributes a
top, a bottom and a right side --- $3$ matches each. Total:
$3n + 1$; the ``$+1$'' is the very first vertical stick. Check:
$n = 1, 2, 3, 4$ give $4, 7, 10, 13$.

\textbf{4.} $4 + 3(n - 1) = 4 + 3n - 3 = 3n + 1$: Amir's
formula expands to exactly the same reduced expression. Full
agreement.

\textbf{5.} Lena: $2n + (n + 1) = 3n + 1$ --- the same again.
Three ways of seeing, one reduced form. Figure $100$:
$3 \times 100 + 1 = 301$ matches.

\textbf{6.} Counts: $3$, $5$, $7$ matches. Each new triangle
leans on an existing side and adds only $2$ matches, starting
from $3$: figure $n$ needs $3 + 2(n - 1) = 2n + 1$ matches.

\textbf{7.} Undo $2n + 1 = 49$: removing the first match leaves
$48$, and each triangle then accounts for $2$:
$48 \div 2 = 24$ triangles.

\textbf{8.} In a row of $n$ tables, each table offers its top
and bottom side ($2n$ seats), and the two end tables offer one
extra side each ($+2$): $2n + 2$ seats. Check: $4$, $6$, $8$
for $n = 1, 2, 3$.

\textbf{9.} One row: $2n + 2 \geq 30$ needs $2n \geq 28$, so
$14$ tables. Splitting the same $14$ tables into two rows of
$7$: each row seats $2 \times 7 + 2 = 16$, together $32$ ---
two more than the single row, because every new row brings two
new \emph{ends}. (Each split gains $2$ seats; caterers know.)

\textbf{10.} The L of size $n$ has $n$ dots going up and $n$
going across, the corner dot shared: $n + n - 1 = 2n - 1$ dots.
Figures $1$, $2$, $3$: $1$, $3$, $5$ dots --- the \emph{\omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd
numbers}}. Nested one around the next, the Ls fill an
$n \times n$ square of dots: the story of
\cref{pb:g8:equations:1}.

\textbf{11.} $3(n + 2) - 5 = 3n + 6 - 5 = 3n + 1$: the claim
reduces to the proved formula, so it is right for every $n$ ---
established by algebra, not by examples.

\textbf{12.} Tests: $4 \geq 2$, $9 \geq 3$, $100 \geq 10$: all
pass. But $n = 0.5$ gives $n^2 = 0.25 < 0.5$: the claim fails.
The three tests proved nothing about \emph{all} numbers (they
only failed to find trouble); the single counterexample
\emph{proves} the general claim false
(\cref{met:g7:literal:test}). A letter stands for every number
--- decimals included.

\textbf{13.} Regions: $2$ points $\to 2$; $3$ points $\to 4$;
$4$ points $\to 8$; $5$ points $\to 16$. The pattern whispers
``each point doubles the count'': $32$ regions expected for
$6$ points.

\textbf{14.} A careful count gives $31$ regions --- not $32$.
The doubling conjecture is dead: it survived four checks and
failed the fifth. (This counterexample is famous enough to have
a name, ``the circle-regions trap''; no evenly-spaced shortcuts
--- three chords meeting at one point would merge regions and
spoil the count.)

\textbf{15.} A proof would have needed a \emph{structural}
reason why adding a point doubles the regions --- and there is
none: the doubling was a coincidence of small cases. The
matchstick formulas were safe because each was read off the
construction itself (each square visibly costs $3$ matches,
each table visibly offers $2$ seats): structure, not testing.
Proved prediction: $3 \times 2\,026 + 1 = 6\,079$ matches.
\end{solution}
