\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:2}
Through $(2,1)$ and $(5,10)$: \omterm{def:g10:reffunc:affine}{slope} $m = \frac{10 - 1}{5 - 2} = 3$; then
$1 = 3 \times 2 + p$ gives $p = -5$: $f(x) = 3x - 5$.

Through $(-1,4)$ and $(3,-4)$: \omterm{def:g10:reffunc:affine}{slope} $m = \frac{-4 - 4}{3 - (-1)} =
\frac{-8}{4} = -2$; then $4 = -2 \times (-1) + p$ gives $p = 2$:
$f(x) = -2x + 2$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:3}
$(3.1)^2 < (3.2)^2$: the square increases on positives.

$(-2.7)^2 < (-2.8)^2$: the square decreases on negatives and
$-2.8 < -2.7$.

$\frac{1}{5.1} > \frac{1}{5.2}$: the inverse decreases on positives.

$\sqrt{17} > \sqrt{15}$: the square root increases.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:4}
$x^2 = 16$: two solutions, $x = 4$ and $x = -4$ (the horizontal line
$y = 16$ cuts the parabola twice).

$x^2 \leq 16$: the parabola is below the line between the two
\omterm{def:g10:numbers:interunion}{intersection} points: $x \in \intcc{-4}{4}$.

$x^2 > 9$: the parabola is strictly above $y = 9$ outside
$\intcc{-3}{3}$: $x \in \intoo{-\infty}{-3} \cup \intoo{3}{+\infty}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:5}
For $0 < x < 1$: multiplying $x < 1$ repeatedly by $x$ gives
$x^3 < x^2 < x$, and \cref{prop:g10:reffunc:compare} gives $x < \sqrt x$,
so
\[
x^3 < x^2 < x < \sqrt x .
\]
Check with $x = 0.25$: $x^3 = 0.015625$, $x^2 = 0.0625$, $x = 0.25$,
$\sqrt x = 0.5$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:6}
$\frac1x = 2$ has the unique solution $x = \frac12$ (the hyperbola meets
the horizontal line $y = 2$ once, on the positive branch).

For $x > 0$: $\frac1x < 2$. Multiplying by $x > 0$: $1 < 2x$, so
$x > \frac12$: solution set $\intoo{\frac12}{+\infty}$.

If $x < 0$ is allowed: every negative $x$ satisfies $\frac1x < 0 < 2$,
so the full solution set is
$\intoo{-\infty}{0} \cup \intoo{\frac12}{+\infty}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:7}
(a) On $\intcc{2}{5}$, all inputs are positive and squaring increases:
$4 \leq x^2 \leq 25$.

(b) On $\intcc{-3}{-1}$, squaring decreases:
the largest square comes from $-3$: $1 \leq x^2 \leq 9$.

(c) On $\intcc{-2}{3}$, the \omterm{def:g10:numbers:interval}{interval} contains $0$, where the square
reaches its \omterm{def:g10:functions:extrema}{minimum} $0$; the \omterm{def:g10:functions:extrema}{maximum} is the larger of $(-2)^2 = 4$ and
$3^2 = 9$. So $0 \leq x^2 \leq 9$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:8}
First company: $f(x) = 1.5x + 4$; second: $g(x) = 2x$. The first is
cheaper when
\[
1.5x + 4 < 2x
\ \Longleftrightarrow\
4 < 0.5x
\ \Longleftrightarrow\
x > 8 .
\]
Beyond $8$ km, the first company is cheaper; at exactly $8$ km both charge
$16$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:9}
Both sides are nonnegative, so the inequality is equivalent to the one
between their squares:
\[
\left(\sqrt{a+b}\right)^2 = a + b
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\left(\sqrt a + \sqrt b\right)^2 = a + 2\sqrt a \sqrt b + b .
\]
Since $2\sqrt a\sqrt b \geq 0$, the second square is at least the first,
which proves $\sqrt{a+b} \leq \sqrt a + \sqrt b$. Equality holds exactly
when $\sqrt a \sqrt b = 0$, i.e.\ when $a = 0$ or $b = 0$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:reffunc:10}
\emph{1.} Put the right-hand side over a common denominator:
\[
2 + \frac{3}{x-1} = \frac{2(x-1) + 3}{x - 1} = \frac{2x + 1}{x - 1}
= f(x).
\]

\emph{2.} On $\intoo{1}{+\infty}$, as $x$ increases, $x - 1$ increases
and stays positive, so $\frac{3}{x-1}$ decreases (inverse \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function}, times
the positive constant $3$), so $f(x) = 2 + \frac{3}{x-1}$ decreases:
$f$ is strictly \omterm{def:g10:functions:variations}{decreasing} on $\intoo{1}{+\infty}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:g10:reffunc:1}
\textbf{1.} $d(30) = 3^2 = 9$ m; $d(50) = 25$ m; $d(90) =
81$ m; $d(130) = 169$ m.

\textbf{2.} Doubling $v$ doubles $\frac v{10}$ and multiplies
its square by $4$: $d(100) = 100$ m $= 4 \times d(50)$. Twice
the speed, four times the distance --- the square \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function}'s
scaling.

\textbf{3.} $\left(\frac{v}{10}\right)^2 = 50$ gives
$\frac{v}{10} = \sqrt{50}$, so $v = 10\sqrt{50} \approx 71$
km/h. The square root answered; the reading is unambiguous
because speeds are positive, and on
$\intco{0}{+\infty}$ the square \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function} climbs strictly
(\cref{prop:g10:reffunc:sqrt}: one positive \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{preimage}).

\textbf{4.} $d(31) - d(30) = 9.61 - 9 = 0.61$ m, while
$d(91) - d(90) = 82.81 - 81 = 1.81$ m: the same $+1$ km/h costs
three times more distance at high speed. The parabola not only
rises, it \emph{steepens} --- its rate of climb grows with $x$.

\textbf{5.} $D(50) = 14 + 25 = 39$ m; $D(130) = 36.4 + 169 =
205.4$ m. In town the affine (reaction) term is the bigger
share; on the highway the square term crushes it --- affine
grows steadily, squares run away.

\textbf{6.} $F(0.5) = 240$, $F(1) = 120$, $F(3) = 40$.

\textbf{7.} The inverse \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function} decreases: the further from
the pivot, the smaller the needed force --- with a long enough
lever arm ($d$ huge), any force, however small, balances any
load: Archimedes' boast lives in the hyperbola's tail, which
approaches $0$ without reaching it. And $d = 0$ is the
forbidden value: no arm, no lever --- the vertical asymptote.

\textbf{8.} $\frac{100}{4} = 25$ at $2$ m,
$\frac{100}{9} \approx 11$ at $3$ m, $1$ at $10$ m. At distance
$d$ the light has spread over a sphere of area $4\pi d^2$
(Archimedes' tombstone problem, in the Middle School volume): the same energy divided by an area
growing like $d^2$ --- hence the inverse \emph{square}.

\textbf{9.} $60$, $30$, $20$ euros per head for $n = 10, 20,
30$. For $\frac{600}{n} \leq 25$: $n \geq 24$ participants. The
hyperbola promises ever-cheaper shares as $n$ grows --- and
refuses ever to reach $0$: the bus is never free.

\textbf{10.} $a(x) = \frac{2x + 3}{x} = 2 + \frac3x$:
\omterm{def:g10:functions:variations}{decreasing} on $\intoo{0}{+\infty}$ (inverse \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function} shifted),
approaching the asymptote $y = 2$. Economically: spreading the
fixed $3$ euros over more posters pushes the unit cost down
towards the incompressible $2$ euros of materials --- economies
of scale, with a hard floor.

\textbf{11.} Earth: $a^3 = 1$, $T^2 = 1$. Mars:
$1.52^3 \approx 3.51$ and $1.88^2 \approx 3.53$. Jupiter:
$5.20^3 \approx 140.6$ and $11.86^2 \approx 140.7$. Kepler's
notice: $T^2 = a^3$ for every planet --- one law for the whole
sky.

\textbf{12.} $T = \sqrt{a^3}$: for Saturn
$\sqrt{9.54^3} = \sqrt{868} \approx 29.5$ years, against the
observed $29.4$: the law holds to a tenth.

\textbf{13.} Asteroid: $T = \sqrt{4^3} = \sqrt{64} = 8$ years.
Comet: $a^3 = 27^2 = 729 = 9^3$, so $a = 9$ AU.

\textbf{14.} Square ($T^2$), cube ($a^3$) and square root (to
extract $T$). Scaling: multiplying $a$ by $4$ multiplies $T$ by
$4\sqrt4 = 8$ --- as the asteroid confirms ($a$ four times
Earth's, $T$ eight times).

\textbf{15.} A table of numbers, matched against the shapes of
a few reference \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{functions}, yielded a law of the universe
seventy years before anyone knew \emph{why} it held ---
recognizing the \omterm{def:g10:functions:function}{function} is half of science.

\textbf{16.} On $\intoo{0}{1}$: $x^2 < x < \sqrt x$; on
$\intoo{1}{+\infty}$: $\sqrt x < x < x^2$. Checks: at
$x = 0.25$: $0.0625 < 0.25 < 0.5$; at $x = 4$:
$2 < 4 < 16$.

\textbf{17.} For $x \geq 0$, both sides are $\geq 0$, so
$\sqrt x > x \iff x > x^2 \iff x(1 - x) > 0 \iff
x \in \intoo{0}{1}$.

\textbf{18.} At $x = 0.5$: $x^3 = 0.125 < x^2 = 0.25 < x =
0.5$. At $x = 2$: $2 < 4 < 8$: the order reverses. Crossings of
$x^2$ and $x^3$: $x^3 - x^2 = x^2(x - 1) = 0$: at $x = 0$ and
$x = 1$.

\textbf{19.} $10t^2 = 100\sqrt t$ gives $t^2 = 10\sqrt t$;
squaring, $t^4 = 100t$, so $t^3 = 100$ and
$t = \sqrt[3]{100} \approx 4.64$ years --- about $4$ years and
$8$ months. (Both schemes then pay about $215$ euros.) Moral:
roots sprint early, powers win every marathon.

\textbf{20.} Braking: the parabola's \emph{steepening} is the
danger of speed. Lever: the \emph{vertical asymptote} at $0$
and the long tail are the mechanic's advantage. Lamp: the
$d^2$ of the \emph{spreading sphere} sets the fading. Kepler:
the \emph{exponent} $\frac32$ is the solar system's tempo.
Savings A: the root's \emph{flattening} is the slow saver's
fate. Five \omterm{def:g10:functions:graph}{graphs}, five laws: nature's alphabet indeed.
\end{solution}
