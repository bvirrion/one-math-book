\begin{solution}{pb:g8:fractions:1}
\textbf{1.} $\frac12 + \frac13 = \frac36 + \frac26 = \frac56$ and
$\frac12 + \frac14 = \frac24 + \frac14 = \frac34$. So
$\frac56 = \frac12 + \frac13$ and $\frac34 = \frac12 + \frac14$
are Egyptian writings (distinct unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fractions} in each).

\textbf{2.} With the common \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $28$:
\[
\frac14 + \frac{1}{28} = \frac{7}{28} + \frac{1}{28}
= \frac{8}{28} = \frac{2}{7} .
\]

\textbf{3.} $\frac13 + \frac16 = \frac26 + \frac16 = \frac36 =
\frac12$, and $\frac14 + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{3}{12} +
\frac{1}{12} = \frac{4}{12} = \frac13$. In general, the common
\omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} of $\frac{1}{n+1}$ and $\frac{1}{n(n+1)}$ is
$n(n+1)$:
\[
\frac{1}{n+1} + \frac{1}{n(n+1)}
= \frac{n}{n(n+1)} + \frac{1}{n(n+1)}
= \frac{n+1}{n(n+1)}
= \frac{1}{n} .
\]

\textbf{4.} Start from $1 = \frac12 + \frac12$ and split the
second \omterm{def:g3:division:half}{half} by question 3:
\[
1 = \frac12 + \frac13 + \frac16 ,
\]
three distinct unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fractions}.

\textbf{5.} Splitting the $\frac13$ in $\frac56 = \frac12 +
\frac13$ gives
\[
\frac56 = \frac12 + \frac14 + \frac{1}{12} .
\]
No Egyptian writing is ever unique: the splitting identity can
always replace the unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fraction} with the largest \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} by
two new, \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{even} smaller unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fractions} --- so from any writing one
manufactures another.

\textbf{6.} With \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $21$: $\frac27 = \frac{6}{21} <
\frac{7}{21} = \frac13$. With \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $28$: $\frac14 =
\frac{7}{28} < \frac{8}{28} = \frac27$. Unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fractions} shrink as
their \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominators} grow, $1 > \frac12 > \frac13 > \frac14 >
\dots$; since $\frac13$ (and everything above it) is too big and
$\frac14$ fits, $\frac14$ is the largest unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fraction} smaller
than $\frac27$.

\textbf{7.} $\frac27 - \frac14 = \frac{8}{28} - \frac{7}{28} =
\frac{1}{28}$: the greedy method produces
$\frac27 = \frac14 + \frac{1}{28}$, exactly the scribes' entry of
question 2.

\textbf{8.} With \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $14$: $\frac12 = \frac{7}{14} <
\frac{10}{14} = \frac57$, while the only larger unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fraction},
$1$, exceeds $\frac57$. So $\frac12$ is the largest that fits,
and
\[
\frac57 - \frac12 = \frac{10}{14} - \frac{7}{14} = \frac{3}{14} .
\]

\textbf{9.} With \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $28$: $\frac{3}{14} = \frac{6}{28} <
\frac{7}{28} = \frac14$, so $\frac14$ is too big. With
\omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $70$: $\frac15 = \frac{14}{70} < \frac{15}{70} =
\frac{3}{14}$, so $\frac15$ fits: it is the largest unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fraction}
smaller than $\frac{3}{14}$. Then
\[
\frac{3}{14} - \frac15 = \frac{15}{70} - \frac{14}{70}
= \frac{1}{70},
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad
\frac57 = \frac12 + \frac15 + \frac{1}{70} .
\]
Check: $\frac{35}{70} + \frac{14}{70} + \frac{1}{70} =
\frac{50}{70} = \frac57$.

\textbf{10.} First step: $\frac12 = \frac{5}{10} < \frac{8}{10} =
\frac45$ and $1 > \frac45$, so subtract $\frac12$:
$\frac45 - \frac12 = \frac{8}{10} - \frac{5}{10} = \frac{3}{10}$.
Second step: $\frac13 = \frac{10}{30} > \frac{9}{30} =
\frac{3}{10}$ is too big, and $\frac14 = \frac{5}{20} <
\frac{6}{20} = \frac{3}{10}$ fits, so subtract $\frac14$:
$\frac{3}{10} - \frac14 = \frac{6}{20} - \frac{5}{20} =
\frac{1}{20}$. Conclusion:
\[
\frac45 = \frac12 + \frac14 + \frac{1}{20},
\qquad
\frac{10}{20} + \frac{5}{20} + \frac{1}{20} = \frac{16}{20}
= \frac45 . \checkmark
\]

\textbf{11.} The \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{numerators} of the successive \omterm{def:g3:division:remainder}{remainders} are:
for $\frac27$: $2$, then $1$; for $\frac57$: $5$, then $3$, then
$1$; for $\frac45$: $4$, then $3$, then $1$. Each list is
\emph{strictly decreasing}.

\textbf{12.} The common \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} of $\frac ab$ and $\frac1n$
is $b \times n$:
\[
\frac{a}{b} - \frac{1}{n}
= \frac{a \times n}{b \times n} - \frac{b}{b \times n}
= \frac{a \times n - b}{b \times n} .
\]

\textbf{13.} Over the common \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $b \times (n-1)$, the
inequality $\frac{1}{n-1} > \frac ab$ compares the \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{numerators}
$b$ and $a \times (n - 1)$:
\[
b > a \times (n - 1) = a \times n - a ,
\]
and adding $a - b$ to both sides gives $a > a \times n - b$. So
after each greedy step the \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{numerator} of the \omterm{def:g3:division:remainder}{remainder} (question
12) is a whole number strictly smaller than the one before. Whole
numbers cannot decrease strictly forever: after at most $a$ steps
the \omterm{def:g3:division:remainder}{remainder} has \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{numerator} $1$ --- a unit \omterm{def:g6:fractions:def}{fraction} --- or is
$0$, and the method stops.

\textbf{14.} With the common \omterm{def:g4:fractions:def}{denominator} $18$:
\[
\frac12 + \frac13 + \frac19
= \frac{9}{18} + \frac{6}{18} + \frac{2}{18}
= \frac{17}{18} ,
\]
which is \emph{less than} $1$: the will forgot $\frac{1}{18}$ of
the herd. That gap is the whole trick. Out of $18$ camels the
shares $\frac12$, $\frac13$, $\frac19$ are the whole numbers $9$,
$6$ and $2$, which add up to $9 + 6 + 2 = 17$: exactly the herd,
and the eighteenth camel goes home. And nobody can complain: the
will promised, out of $17$ camels, $\frac{17}{2} = 8.5$, then
$\frac{17}{3} \approx 5.67$, then $\frac{17}{9} \approx 1.89$
camels, and each child received strictly more ($9$, $6$ and $2$).
The forgotten $\frac{1}{18}$ is what the neighbour's camel
temporarily filled.

\textbf{15.} Question 4 provides the repaired will:
\[
\frac12 + \frac13 + \frac16 = 1 .
\]
The shares are whole numbers when the herd size is \omterm{def:g6:wholes:divisible}{divisible} by
$2$, by $3$ and by $6$ --- that is, \omterm{def:g6:wholes:divisible}{divisible} by $6$. The
smallest such herd is $6$ camels: shares $3$, $2$ and $1$, and
nothing is left over.
\end{solution}
