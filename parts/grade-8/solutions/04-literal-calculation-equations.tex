\begin{solution}{exo:g8:equations:12}
Multiply both sides by $20$:
\[
5(x + 3) = 4(2x - 1)
\ \Longrightarrow\
5x + 15 = 8x - 4
\ \Longrightarrow\
19 = 3x
\ \Longrightarrow\
x = \frac{19}{3}.
\]
Check: $\frac{19/3 + 3}{4} = \frac{28/3}{4} = \frac{28}{12} =
\frac73$, and $\frac{2 \times 19/3 - 1}{5} = \frac{35/3}{5} =
\frac{35}{15} = \frac73$. Equal: the solution is $\frac{19}{3}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:g8:equations:1}
\textbf{1.} Double distributivity
(\cref{thm:g8:equations:expand}) with $c = a$, $d = b$:
\[
(a + b)(a + b) = a^2 + ab + ba + b^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2 ,
\]
since $ab$ and $ba$ are the same \omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product}.

\textbf{2.} Same expansion, minding the signs:
\[
(a - b)(a - b) = a^2 - ab - ba + b^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2 ,
\]
\[
(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - ab + ba - b^2 = a^2 - b^2 :
\]
this time the cross-terms $-ab$ and $+ba$ cancel instead of
doubling.

\textbf{3.} The square of side $a + b$ cuts into four pieces: a
square $a \times a$ (the $a^2$), a square $b \times b$ (the
$b^2$), and \emph{two} rectangles $a \times b$ (together, the
$2ab$). The identity $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ is the statement
that the four \omterm{def:g6:measure:area}{areas} fill the big square --- it is the picture of
double distributivity with both sides split the same way.

\textbf{4.} With the identities:
\begin{align*}
101^2 &= (100 + 1)^2 = 10\,000 + 2 \times 100 + 1 = 10\,201, \\
99^2 &= (100 - 1)^2 = 10\,000 - 200 + 1 = 9\,801, \\
49 \times 51 &= (50 - 1)(50 + 1) = 50^2 - 1^2 = 2\,499 .
\end{align*}

\textbf{5.} For $a = 3$, $b = 4$: $(3 + 4)^2 = 49$ but
$3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$. Zoe's formula forgets the two
$a \times b$ rectangles of the picture --- the missing
$2ab = 24$, and indeed $25 + 24 = 49$.

\textbf{6.} $1$; $1 + 3 = 4$; $1 + 3 + 5 = 9$;
$1 + 3 + 5 + 7 = 16$; $1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 9 = 25$. The results are
the perfect squares $1^2, 2^2, 3^2, 4^2, 5^2$.

\textbf{7.} The \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd numbers} start at $1$ and go up in steps of
$2$: the $n$-th one is reached after $n - 1$ steps of $2$ from
$1$, so it equals $1 + 2(n - 1) = 2n - 1$. Check: $n = 1, 2, 3$
give $1, 3, 5$.

\textbf{8.} By the first identity,
\[
(n + 1)^2 - n^2 = n^2 + 2n + 1 - n^2 = 2n + 1 :
\]
the \omterm{ex:g1:subtraction:difference}{difference} between two consecutive squares is exactly the
$(n+1)$-th \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd number} (question 7 with $n + 1$ in place of $n$:
$2(n+1) - 1 = 2n + 1$).

\textbf{9.} Build the squares one after the other. Start with a
single cell: $1 = 1^2$. To pass from the square of side $1$ to
the square of side $2$, add $2 \times 1 + 1 = 3$ cells (question
8): $1 + 3 = 2^2$. To pass to side $3$, add $2 \times 2 + 1 = 5$
cells: $1 + 3 + 5 = 3^2$. Each new \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd number} is exactly the
L-shaped layer completing the next square, so after adding the
first $n$ \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd numbers} the square of side $n$ is complete:
$1 + 3 + \dots + (2n - 1) = n^2$.

\textbf{10.} The \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd numbers} from $1$ to $99$ are the first $50$
of them ($2n - 1 = 99$ gives $n = 50$), so
\[
1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + 99 = 50^2 = 2\,500 .
\]
And $1 + 3 + \dots + (2n - 1) = 400 = 20^2$ requires $n = 20$:
the first \emph{twenty} \omterm{def:g3:numbers:evenodd}{odd numbers} ($1$ up to $39$) add up to
$400$.

\textbf{11.} $x^2 + 6x + 9 = x^2 + 2 \times 3 \times x + 3^2 =
(x + 3)^2$: the first identity with $a = x$, $b = 3$. So the
\omterm{def:g8:equations:def}{equation} reads $(x + 3)^2 = 0$. A square is \omterm{def:g1:counting:zero}{zero} only when the
number itself is \omterm{def:g1:counting:zero}{zero} (\cref{thm:g8:negprod:rules}: a nonzero
number has nonzero distance, so its square has nonzero distance
too). Hence $x + 3 = 0$: the only solution is $x = -3$.

\textbf{12.} By \cref{thm:g8:negprod:rules}, the distance of a
\omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product} is the \omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product} of the distances. If both factors are
nonzero, both distances are nonzero, so the \omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product}'s distance is
nonzero: the \omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product} cannot be $0$. A \omterm{def:g1:counting:zero}{zero} \omterm{def:g2:mult:def}{product} therefore
forces at least one \omterm{def:g1:counting:zero}{zero} factor. For $(x - 2)(x + 5) = 0$: either
$x - 2 = 0$ or $x + 5 = 0$, giving the two solutions $x = 2$ and
$x = -5$.

\textbf{13.} $x^2 = 49$ becomes $x^2 - 49 = 0$, and the third
identity factors it:
\[
x^2 - 49 = x^2 - 7^2 = (x - 7)(x + 7) = 0 .
\]
By question 12, $x = 7$ or $x = -7$: \emph{two} solutions. Every
\omterm{def:g8:equations:def}{equation} solved so far in this chapter was of first degree and
had exactly one solution; a square in the \omterm{def:g8:equations:def}{equation} can produce
two.

\textbf{14.} Bring everything to the left side:
$x^2 - 6x + 9 = 0$, and recognize the second identity:
$x^2 - 2 \times 3 \times x + 3^2 = (x - 3)^2$. So the \omterm{def:g8:equations:def}{equation} is
$(x - 3)^2 = 0$, which forces $x - 3 = 0$ (as in question 11):
$x = 3$ is the one and only solution --- the check of
\cref{exo:g8:equations:3} found it, and the factoring proves
there are no others.

\textbf{15.} By the third identity,
\[
2026^2 - 2025^2 = (2026 - 2025)(2026 + 2025)
= 1 \times 4051 = 4\,051 .
\]
In general $(n+1)^2 - n^2 = (n + 1 - n)(n + 1 + n) = 2n + 1$: the
\omterm{ex:g1:subtraction:difference}{difference} of two consecutive squares is the \omterm{def:g1:addition:def}{sum} of the two
numbers --- the same $2n + 1$ as in question 8, now obtained from
the third identity instead of the first.
\end{solution}
