\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:1}
(a) $\bar z$: $P = x$, $Q = -y$, so $P_x = 1 \neq -1 = Q_y$:
nowhere $\C$-differentiable. $\abs z^2$: $P = x^2 + y^2$, $Q =
0$: Cauchy--Riemann demands $2x = 0 = 2y$: differentiable at
$0$ only --- and \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} nowhere (no \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{open set}).
$\operatorname{Re}z$: $P_x = 1 \neq 0 = Q_y$: nowhere.

(b) $x^2 - y^2 = \operatorname{Re}(z^2)$: $f(z) = z^2$ works.
All solutions: if $\operatorname{Re}f = \operatorname{Re}g$
with $f, g$ \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} $\C$, then $h = f -
g$ has $\operatorname{Re}h = 0$; Cauchy--Riemann gives $h' =
P_x + \iu Q_x = 0 - \iu P_y = 0$: $h$ is an imaginary
constant. Answer: $f(z) = z^2 + \iu c$, $c \in \R$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:2}
On the unit circle $\gamma(t) = \eu^{\iu t}$:
\[
\int_C z^n\,\dd z = \int_0^{2\pi}\eu^{\iu nt}\,\iu\eu^{\iu
t}\dd t = \iu\int_0^{2\pi}\eu^{\iu(n+1)t}\dd t
= \begin{cases} 2\iu\pi & n = -1,\\ 0 & n \neq -1.\end{cases}
\]
For $\bar z$: along $[0, 1+\iu]$, $\gamma(t) = t(1 + \iu)$:
$\int_0^1 t(1 - \iu)(1 + \iu)\dd t = \int_0^12t\,\dd t = 1$.
Along $0 \to 1 \to 1 + \iu$: $\int_0^1t\dd t +
\int_0^1(1 - \iu t)\,\iu\,\dd t = \frac12 + \iu + \frac12 = 1
+ \iu$. Different values between the same endpoints: by
\cref{prop:b3:holomorphic:primitive}, $\bar z$ has no
primitive on any \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{open set} containing both \omterm{def:b3:topology:pathconnected}{paths}.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:3}
(a) The cut plane $\Omega = \C\setminus\intoc{-\infty}0$ is
star-shaped about $1$, and $\frac1z \in \mathcal H(\Omega)$:
\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:cauchy} provides a primitive $L$
with $L(1) = 0$. Then $\bigl(z\eu^{-L(z)}\bigr)' =
\eu^{-L}(1 - z\cdot\frac1z) = 0$: $z = c\,\eu^{L(z)}$ with $c
= 1$ (at $z = 1$). Writing $L = u + \iu v$: $\abs z = \eu^u$
and $z = \abs z\eu^{\iu v}$ with $v$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}, $v(1) = 0$,
$v \in \intoo{-\pi}\pi$ ($v$ is a \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} argument of $z$
on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} $\Omega$, so its image avoids the odd
multiples of $\pi$ --- no point of $\Omega$ lies on $\R_-$
--- and, containing $v(1) = 0$, stays in $\intoo{-\pi}\pi$:
$v$ is the principal argument): $L = \log$.

(b) If $g$ were a \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} logarithm on $\C^*$: $h(t) =
g(\eu^{\iu t})$ satisfies $\eu^{h(t)} = \eu^{\iu t}$, so $h(t)
- \iu t \in 2\iu\pi\Z$, and by \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} $h(t) = \iu t +
2\iu\pi k$ for a fixed integer $k$. Then $g(1) = h(0) =
2\iu\pi k$ and $g(1) = h(2\pi) = 2\iu\pi(k + 1)$:
contradiction.

(c) On $D(0,1)$: $\log(1 + z) =
\sum_{n\geq1}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}z^n$ --- both sides vanish at
$0$ and have derivative $\frac1{1+z} = \sum(-1)^nz^n$
(\cref{ex:b3:holomorphic:examples}); a primitive is unique up
to a constant on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} disc.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:4}
(a) Expand at $0$ (radius $\infty$): by the \omterm{thm:b3:holomorphic:analytic}{Cauchy estimates}
on $C_r$, $\abs{c_k} \leq C(1 + r)^n/r^k \to 0$ as $r \to
\infty$ for $k > n$: $f = \sum_{k\leq n}c_kz^k$.

(b) If $\operatorname{Re}f \leq M$: $g = \eu^f$ is entire with
$\abs g = \eu^{\operatorname{Re}f} \leq \eu^M$: constant by
Liouville. Then $g' = f'g = 0$ with $g$ nonvanishing: $f' =
0$, and $f$ is constant.

(c) If $f$ omits the half-plane $H$, an affine map $w \mapsto
\alpha w + \beta$ sends $\C\setminus H$ into
$\{\operatorname{Re} \leq M\}$; apply (b) to $\alpha f +
\beta$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:5}
(a) $g(z) = f(z) - z^2$ vanishes at the points $\frac1n$,
which accumulate at $0 \in \Omega$: by the identity theorem
($\Omega$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}), $g \equiv 0$: $f(z) = z^2$.

(b) Yes: $f(z) = z\cos(\pi/z)$ is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on $\C^*$
(composition) and $f(\frac1n) = \frac1n\cos(n\pi) =
\frac{(-1)^n}n$. No contradiction with (a): the accumulation
point $0$ of the interpolation nodes does \emph{not} belong to
$\C^*$, so the identity theorem is silent --- two distinct
functions ($z\cos(\pi/z)$ and, say, the one from another
interpolation) may share these values.

(c) $f \equiv 0$ everywhere, versus $g = 0$ on $D(0,1)$ and
$g = 1$ on $D(3,1)$: \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on the disconnected union,
equal on $D(0,1)$, different. The identity theorem's
open-closed argument needs \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connectedness} to propagate from one
component to the other --- and cannot.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:6}
(a) By the maximum principle applied on the bounded domain:
$\sup_{\mathbb D}\abs f = \sup_{\partial\mathbb D}\abs f = 1$.
Since $f$ has no zeros, $1/f$ is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on $\mathbb D$,
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{closure}, with boundary modulus $1$: likewise
$\abs{1/f} \leq 1$, i.e.\ $\abs f \geq 1$. So $\abs f \equiv
1$: the modulus attains an \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior} maximum, and
\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:maximum}(2) forces $f$ constant.

(b) $f(z) = z$: boundary modulus $1$, zero at the origin,
nonconstant --- the zero is exactly what blocks the $1/f$
argument.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:7}
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{Continuity} of $G$: dominated convergence with dominator $h$
(locally uniform bound). \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{Holomorphy} by Morera (established
inside \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:weierstrassconv}): for a
closed triangle $T$ in a disc where $\abs g \leq h$,
\[
\int_{\partial T}G(z)\,\dd z =
\int_X\Bigl(\int_{\partial T}g(x, z)\,\dd z\Bigr)\dd\mu(x) =
0,
\]
the interchange by Fubini ($\int_X\int_{\partial T}\abs
g \leq \operatorname{length}(\partial T)\int h < \infty$) and
the inner vanishing by Goursat. For $\Gamma$: on the strip $a
\leq \operatorname{Re}z \leq b$ ($0 < a \leq b$),
$\abs{t^{z-1}\eu^{-t}} = t^{\operatorname{Re}z-1}\eu^{-t} \leq
(t^{a-1} + t^{b-1})\eu^{-t}$, \omterm{def:b3:lebesgue:l1}{integrable} on
$\intoo0{+\infty}$: $\Gamma$ is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on
$\{\operatorname{Re} z > 0\}$ (the \omterm{def:b3:measure:measure}{measure} is only
$\sigma$-finite, but the argument needs only the \omterm{def:b3:lebesgue:l1}{integrable}
dominator). By the identity theorem, the functional equation
$\Gamma(z + 1) = z\Gamma(z)$, proved on
$\intoo0{+\infty}$ (\cref{ex:b3:lebesgue:gamma}), holds on the
whole half-plane.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:8}
Write $P = c\prod_k(X - a_k)^{m_k}$
(\cref{pb:b3:holomorphic:1}). Let $P'(z) = 0$. If $P(z) = 0$,
then $z$ is one of the $a_k$: in the hull. Otherwise, the
logarithmic derivative gives
\[
0 = \frac{P'(z)}{P(z)} = \sum_k\frac{m_k}{z - a_k}
= \sum_k m_k\,\frac{\bar z - \bar a_k}{\abs{z - a_k}^2} ;
\]
conjugating, $\sum_kw_k(z - a_k) = 0$ with $w_k = m_k/\abs{z -
a_k}^2 > 0$: $z = \sum_k\frac{w_k}{\sum w}\,a_k$, a convex
combination of the roots. For $P = z^3 - 1$: roots the cube
roots of unity, $P' = 3z^2$ with double root $0$ --- the
centroid of the equilateral triangle.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:9}
The closed unit square $K = \{x + \iu y : 0 \leq x, y \leq
1\}$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}: $M = \sup_K\abs f < \infty$. Every $z \in \C$
differs from a point of $K$ by an element of $\Z + \iu\Z$
(subtract integer parts), and $f$ is invariant under those
translations (iterate the two relations): $\abs f \leq M$ on
$\C$. Liouville: $f$ is constant. Hence any nonconstant
doubly periodic meromorphic function --- the elliptic
functions of the classical theory --- must have poles.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:10}
(a) Take real parts in the mean value formula
(\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:maximum}(1)). Smoothness: $f$ is
analytic, so $P, Q \in \mathcal C^\infty$; differentiating
Cauchy--Riemann: $P_{xx} = (Q_y)_x = (Q_x)_y = (-P_y)_y =
-P_{yy}$ (Schwarz symmetry of second derivatives): $\Delta P =
0$.

(b) If $\operatorname{Re}f$ attained an \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior} maximum on a
\omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} $\Omega$: $g = \eu^f$ has $\abs g =
\eu^{\operatorname{Re}f}$ attaining an \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior} maximum, so
$g$, hence $\operatorname{Re}f = \ln\abs g$, is constant
(\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:maximum}(2)). On a bounded domain
with \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} up to the boundary,
$\sup_{\bar\Omega}\operatorname{Re}f =
\sup_{\partial\Omega}\operatorname{Re}f$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:11}
(a) The two formulas agree on $I$ ($z = \bar z$ and $f$ real
there: $\overline{f(\bar z)} = \overline{f(z)} = f(z)$), and
$z \mapsto \overline{f(\bar z)}$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on
$\Omega^-\cup I$ as a composition of \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous maps}: $F$ is
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on $\Omega$. \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{Holomorphy} on $\Omega^-$: near $z_0
\in \Omega^-$, expand $f(w) = \sum c_n(w - \bar z_0)^n$ near
$\bar z_0 \in \Omega^+$; then
\[
\overline{f(\bar z)} = \sum_n\bar c_n\,(z - z_0)^n,
\]
a convergent power series: \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic}.

(b) Triangles avoiding $I$ are handled by Goursat in
$\Omega^\pm$. For a triangle meeting $I$, cut it by the real
axis into at most three triangles/quadrilaterals, each with
one side on $I$; for such a piece $P$ contained in, say,
$\overline{\Omega^+}$, the \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:contour}{contour integral} is the limit as
$\varepsilon \to 0^+$ of the integral over $P + \iu
\varepsilon$-type translates (uniform \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} of $F$ on
the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} piece makes the boundary integrals converge, the
side on $I$ being approached from above), and each translate
lies in $\Omega^+$ where Goursat gives $0$. Summing the
pieces: $\int_{\partial T}F = 0$. Morera (the criterion
inside \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:weierstrassconv}): $F$ is
\omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on $\Omega$.

(c) On the disc, $G(z) = \overline{f(\bar z)}$ is
\omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} by (a)'s computation and agrees with $f$ on the
diameter, a set with accumulation points: $G = f$ everywhere
(identity theorem). If $f$ were real on a nonempty \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{open set}
$U$: on $U$ both partials of $Q = \operatorname{Im}f$
vanish, and Cauchy--Riemann transfers this to $P =
\operatorname{Re}f$ ($P_x = Q_y = 0$, $P_y = -Q_x = 0$), so
$f' = P_x + \iu Q_x = 0$ on $U$: $f$ is constant on $U$,
hence everywhere by the identity theorem ($\Omega$
\omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}).
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:holomorphic:12}
(a) $1 = f^2 + g^2 = (f + \iu g)(f - \iu g)$, so $h = f +
\iu g$ never vanishes (its cofactor would have to blow up).
For zero-free entire $h$: $h'/h$ is entire, and $\C$ is
star-shaped, so it has a primitive $\varphi_0$
(\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:cauchy}); then
$\bigl(h\eu^{-\varphi_0}\bigr)' = \eu^{-\varphi_0}(h' -
h\varphi_0') = 0$: $h = c\,\eu^{\varphi_0}$ with $c \neq 0$,
and absorbing a constant $\log c$ into $\varphi = \varphi_0
+ \log c$ (any complex logarithm of $c$): $h =
\eu^{\varphi}$.

(b) With $h = \eu^{\varphi}$ and $h^{-1} = f - \iu g =
\eu^{-\varphi}$:
\[
f = \frac{\eu^{\varphi} + \eu^{-\varphi}}2,
\qquad
g = \frac{\eu^{\varphi} - \eu^{-\varphi}}{2\iu} .
\]
Writing $\varphi = \iu\psi$ with $\psi = -\iu\varphi$
entire, these read $f = \cos\psi$, $g = \sin\psi$: the
entire solutions are exactly the pairs $(\cos\psi,
\sin\psi)$ with $\psi$ entire, and the converse is the
identity $\cos^2 + \sin^2 = 1$.
For $f^2 + g^2 = 0$: $(f + \iu g)(f - \iu g) = 0$ in the
integral domain $\mathcal H(\C)$ (\,$\C$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}: zero
divisors would violate the identity theorem): $g = \pm\iu
f$ with $f$ arbitrary entire.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:b3:holomorphic:1}
\textbf{1.} For $\abs z \geq 1$:
\[
\abs{P(z)} \geq \abs z^n\Bigl(1 -
\frac{\abs{a_{n-1}}}{\abs z} - \dots -
\frac{\abs{a_0}}{\abs z^n}\Bigr)
\geq \abs z^n\Bigl(1 - \frac{A}{\abs z}\Bigr),
\qquad A = \sum_k\abs{a_k} :
\]
for $\abs z \geq R_0 = \max(1, 2A)$, $\abs{P(z)} \geq
\frac12\abs z^n \to \infty$.

\textbf{2.} Choose $R \geq R_0$ with $\frac12R^n \geq
\abs{P(0)}$. On the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} $\bar D(0, R)$ the \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}
$\abs P$ attains a minimum, at some $z_0$; outside, $\abs P
\geq \frac12R^n \geq \abs{P(0)} \geq \abs{P(z_0)}$: the
minimum is global.

\textbf{3.} $Q(h) = P(z_0 + h)/P(z_0)$ is a polynomial in $h$
with $Q(0) = 1$; it is nonconstant ($P$ is), so some
coefficient beyond the constant is nonzero: $Q(h) = 1 +
c_kh^k + h^{k+1}S(h)$ with $k \geq 1$ minimal, $c_k \neq 0$,
$S \in \C[X]$.

\textbf{4.} Roots: any $w = \rho\eu^{\iu\varphi} \neq 0$ has
the $k$-th root $\rho^{1/k}\eu^{\iu\varphi/k}$, where
$\rho^{1/k}$ exists by the intermediate value theorem applied
to $t \mapsto t^k$ on $\intco0\infty$ --- no circularity.
Pick $\omega$ with $\omega^k = -1/c_k$. Then
\[
Q(t\omega) = 1 - t^k + t^{k+1}\,\omega^{k+1}S(t\omega),
\qquad
\abs{Q(t\omega)} \leq 1 - t^k + C\,t^{k+1}
\quad (0 < t \leq 1),
\]
with $C = \abs\omega^{k+1}\sup_{\abs h \leq
\abs\omega}\abs{S(h)}$ (note $1 - t^k \geq 0$ on $\intcc01$).

\textbf{5.} For $0 < t < \min(1, 1/C)$: $\abs{Q(t\omega)}
\leq 1 - t^k(1 - Ct) < 1$, i.e.\ $\abs{P(z_0 + t\omega)} <
\abs{P(z_0)}$ --- contradicting global minimality. So
$P(z_0) = 0$: d'Alembert--Argand's proof is \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete}.

\textbf{6.} Liouville version: if $P$ never vanishes, $1/P$
is entire; by question 1, $\abs{1/P} \leq 2R_0^{-n}$ outside
$\bar D(0, R_0)$, and $1/P$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on that \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
disc, hence bounded there too: bounded entire, so constant
(\cref{cor:b3:holomorphic:liouville}), making $P$ constant:
absurd. Ingredients: d'Alembert uses compactness (existence
of the minimum) and the polar-form existence of $k$-th roots;
Liouville uses the entire Cauchy apparatus (Goursat ---
itself a \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{nested-compacts} argument --- and the \omterm{thm:b3:holomorphic:analytic}{Cauchy
estimates}) plus the same coercivity. Compactness of closed
discs is the common, \omterm{def:b3:rings:divisibility}{irreducible} core.

\textbf{7.} If $\deg P \geq 1$, pick a root $\alpha$
(questions 5); divide: $P = (X - \alpha)Q + P(\alpha) = (X -
\alpha)Q$, with $\deg Q = n - 1$; induct. Grouping equal
factors: $P = c\prod_i(X - \alpha_i)^{m_i}$, $\sum m_i = n$.

\textbf{8.} For real $P$: $P(\bar\alpha) =
\overline{P(\alpha)} = 0$, and multiplicities agree
(conjugate the factorization): nonreal roots come in pairs,
contributing $(X - \alpha)(X - \bar\alpha) = X^2 -
2\operatorname{Re}(\alpha)X + \abs\alpha^2$, a real quadratic
with discriminant $< 0$. Hence the stated list of
\omterm{def:b3:rings:divisibility}{irreducibles}, and a real polynomial of odd degree, having an
even number of nonreal roots, must have a real one. Direct
proof: $P(x) \to \pm\infty$ as $x \to \pm\infty$ (odd degree,
positive leading coefficient say), so $P$ changes sign, and
the intermediate value theorem applies. For $X^3 - X - 1$:
both arguments give the single real root $\approx 1.3247$
(and a conjugate pair).

\textbf{9.} (i) $\chi_u \in \C[X]$ is nonconstant: it has a
root $\lambda$, and $\det(u - \lambda\,\mathrm{id}) = 0$
gives an eigenvector; the elementary-divisor machinery of
\cref{thm:b3:modules:jordan} then applies to any complex
matrix, $\chi$ always splitting. (ii) Let $P \in \bar\Q[X]$
be nonconstant. As a polynomial over $\C$ it has a root $z
\in \C$; $z$ is \omterm{def:b3:galois:algebraic}{algebraic} over $\bar\Q$, hence over $\Q$ by
transitivity (\cref{cor:b3:galois:algclosed}), so $z \in
\bar\Q$: every nonconstant polynomial over $\bar\Q$ has a
root in $\bar\Q$.

\textbf{10.} Over $\Q(\iu)$, question 4 already fails:
$k$-th roots need not exist (no $\sqrt2$), and even granting
roots, question 2 fails --- a minimizing sequence need not
converge, $\Q$ lacking \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{completeness}; in Liouville's route,
Goursat's nested \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} triangles have empty intersection
over $\Q(\iu)$-points. Both proofs consume the \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{completeness}
(equivalently, via bounded monotone convergence, the
\omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{order-completeness}) of $\R$; \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connectedness} powers the
intermediate value theorem behind polar form. The statement
``$\C$ is \omterm{def:b3:galois:closure}{algebraically closed}'' is algebra; every known
proof of it is analysis smuggled through the definition of
$\R$.

\textbf{11.} By \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:analytic} at $a$,
$f^{(n)}(a) = n!\,c_n$ with
\[
c_n = \frac1{2\iu\pi}\int_{C_r}\frac{f(w)}{(w -
a)^{n+1}}\,\dd w,
\qquad
\abs{c_n} \leq \frac{2\pi r}{2\pi}\cdot
\frac{\sup_{C_r}\abs f}{r^{n+1}}
= \frac{\sup_{C_r}\abs f}{r^{n}} :
\]
the \omterm{thm:b3:holomorphic:analytic}{Cauchy estimates}, in the displayed form after
multiplying by $n!$. If $\abs f \leq M$ on $\C$: for every
$a$ and every $r$, $\abs{f'(a)} \leq M/r \to 0$ as $r \to
\infty$, so $f' \equiv 0$ and $f$ is constant on the
\omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} $\C$ --- Liouville recovered.

\textbf{12.} Expand $f = \sum_kc_kz^k$ at $0$ (radius
$\infty$). For $k > m$: $\abs{c_k} \leq (A + Br^m)/r^k \to
0$ as $r \to \infty$, so $c_k = 0$: $f =
\sum_{k\leq m}c_kz^k$ is a polynomial of degree at most $m$.

\textbf{13.} $g = \eu^f$ is entire with $\abs g =
\eu^{\operatorname{Re}f} \leq \eu^M$: constant by question
11. Then $0 = g' = f'g$ with $g$ zero-free: $f' = 0$ and $f$
is constant.

\textbf{14.} Let $M = \sup_K\abs f$ on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} closed
unit square $K$. Every $z$ differs from a point of $K$ by an
element of $\Z + \iu\Z$ (subtract integer parts), and
iterating the two periodicity relations leaves $f$
unchanged: $\abs f \leq M$ on all of $\C$, and question 11
makes $f$ constant. So a nonconstant function invariant
under the lattice cannot be entire: the elliptic functions
of the classical theory must carry poles --- the historical
gateway to \cref{ch:b3:residues}.

\textbf{15.} If $f(\C)$ misses the disc $D(a, r)$, then
$\abs{f(z) - a} \geq r$ for all $z$, so $g = 1/(f - a)$ is
entire with $\abs g \leq 1/r$: constant by Liouville, hence
$f$ constant. Contrapositive: the range of a nonconstant
entire function meets every disc --- it is dense in $\C$.

\textbf{16.} Choose $R$ with $\abs f \geq 1$ outside
$D(0, R)$. The zeros of $f$ lie in the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
$\bar D(0, R)$; were they infinite, they would accumulate
there, and \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:identity} would force $f
\equiv 0$ --- impossible. Call them $z_1, \dots, z_p$, with
multiplicities $m_1, \dots, m_p$, put $M = \sum m_i$ and
$\Pi(z) = \prod_i(z - z_i)^{m_i}$. Factoring each zero out
of the power series, $g = f/\Pi$ is entire and zero-free.
For $\abs z \geq \max(R, 2\max_i\abs{z_i})$: $\abs{z - z_i}
\leq 2\abs z$ and $\abs f \geq 1$, so $\abs{1/g} =
\abs\Pi/\abs f \leq 2^M\abs z^M$; on the remaining \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
disc $1/g$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}, hence bounded: $\abs{1/g} \leq A +
B\abs z^M$ everywhere. By question 12, $1/g$ is a
polynomial; it is zero-free, so by question 7 it is a
nonzero constant $c$: $f = \frac1c\Pi$ is a polynomial.
Conversely, question 1 makes every nonconstant polynomial
proper. And $\eu^z$ is honestly excluded: along $\R_-$,
$\abs{\eu^z} = \eu^x \to 0$ while $\abs z \to \infty$.

\textbf{17.} Parametrize \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:formula}
at the \omterm{ex:b3:groups:actions}{center}: with $w = a + r\eu^{\iu\theta}$, $\dd w = \iu
r\eu^{\iu\theta}\dd\theta$,
\[
f(a) = \frac1{2\iu\pi}\int_{C_r}\frac{f(w)}{w - a}\,\dd w
= \frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
f\bigl(a + r\eu^{\iu\theta}\bigr)\,\dd\theta ;
\]
taking real parts gives the mean value property of $u$. If
$u$ attains a maximum at an \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior} point of the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}
$\Omega$: $\abs{\eu^f} = \eu^u$ attains an \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior} maximum,
so $\eu^f$ is constant by
\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:maximum}(2), and $u =
\log\abs{\eu^f}$ is constant. On a bounded domain with
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} up to the boundary, $\sup_{\bar\Omega}u =
\sup_{\partial\Omega}u$, exactly as for $\abs f$.

\textbf{18.} \emph{Case $\abs a > r$.} Pick $R$ with $r < R
< \abs a$: on the convex disc $D(0, R)$ the function $a - z$
is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} and zero-free, and $z \mapsto -1/(a - z)$ has
a primitive $L$ there (\cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:cauchy});
after adjusting the constant, $\bigl(\eu^{-L}(a -
z)\bigr)' = \eu^{-L}\bigl(-L'\,(a - z) - 1\bigr) = 0$ gives
$\eu^L = a - z$: a \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} logarithm exists, and
$\log\abs{a - z} = \operatorname{Re}L(z)$. Question 17's
mean value property at $0$, radius $r$:
\[
\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
\log\bigl|a - r\eu^{\iu\theta}\bigr|\,\dd\theta
= \operatorname{Re}L(0) = \log\abs a .
\]
\emph{Case $\abs a < r$.} From $a - r\eu^{\iu\theta} =
-r\eu^{\iu\theta}\bigl(1 - \frac ar\eu^{-\iu\theta}\bigr)$,
the mean equals $\log r$ plus the mean of $\log\abs{1 -
\frac ar\eu^{-\iu\theta}}$. The substitution $\theta \mapsto
2\pi - \theta$, then --- writing $\frac ar =
\rho\eu^{\iu\varphi}$, $\rho < 1$, the case $a = 0$ being
trivial --- the shift $\theta \mapsto \theta - \varphi$
(both preserve means over a period) turn this into the mean
of $\log\abs{1 - \rho\eu^{\iu\theta}}$: the first case with
$(a, r) = (1, \rho)$, which gives $\log 1 = 0$. Total:
$\log r = \log\max(\abs a, r)$ in both cases.

\textbf{19.} $\log\abs{P(\eu^{\iu\theta})} = \log\abs c +
\sum_i\log\abs{\alpha_i - \eu^{\iu\theta}}$, each root
repeated per its multiplicity; averaging in $\theta$ and
applying question 18 with $r = 1$ to each root off the unit
circle yields $\log\bigl(\abs c\prod_i\max(1,
\abs{\alpha_i})\bigr)$. Checks. For $P = 2X - 1 = 2(X -
\frac12)$ the formula predicts $\log 2$; directly,
$\abs{2\eu^{\iu\theta} - 1} = 2\abs{\frac12 -
\eu^{\iu\theta}}$ and the mean of $\log\abs{\frac12 -
\eu^{\iu\theta}}$ is $\log\max(\frac12, 1) = 0$: mean $\log
2$. For $P = X^2 - X = X(X - 1)$ the root $1$ sits \emph{on}
the circle; the formula predicts $0$. Directly, the mean of
$\log\abs{\eu^{\iu\theta}}$ is $0$, and with
$\abs{\eu^{\iu\theta} - 1} = 2\abs{\sin\frac\theta2}$:
\[
\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
\log\Bigl(2\sin\frac\theta2\Bigr)\dd\theta
= \frac1\pi\int_0^\pi\log(2\sin u)\,\dd u
= \log 2 + \frac J\pi,
\qquad J = \int_0^\pi\log\sin u\,\dd u .
\]
The substitution $u = 2v$ and $\sin 2v = 2\sin v\cos v$ give
$J = \pi\log2 + 2\int_0^{\pi/2}\log\sin +
2\int_0^{\pi/2}\log\cos = \pi\log 2 + 2J$ (each half equals
$J/2$ by the symmetries of $\sin$), so $J = -\pi\log 2$ (the
improper integrals converge, $\log\sin$ being \omterm{def:b3:lebesgue:l1}{integrable} at
the endpoints): the mean is $\log2 - \log2 = 0$. The formula
survives roots on the circle.

\textbf{20.} $\frac{\eu^z - 1}z =
\sum_{j\geq0}\frac{z^j}{(j+1)!}$ is entire and equals $1$ at
$0$: its reciprocal is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} near $0$ (on $\abs z <
2\pi$ in fact, the nearest other zeros of $\eu^z - 1$ being
$\pm2\iu\pi$), so $\frac z{\eu^z-1}$ is analytic at $0$.
Multiplying the two series and reading the coefficient of
$z^n$, $n \geq 1$, in $(\frac{\eu^z-1}z)\cdot(\frac
z{\eu^z-1}) = 1$:
\[
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{B_k}{k!\,(n+1-k)!} = 0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n+1}{k}B_k = 0 .
\]
Successively: $B_0 = 1$, $B_1 = -\frac12$, $B_2 = \frac16$,
$B_3 = 0$, $B_4 = -\frac1{30}$, $B_5 = 0$, $B_6 =
\frac1{42}$. Evenness: with $F(z) = \frac z{\eu^z-1} +
\frac z2$,
\[
F(-z) = \frac{-z}{\eu^{-z} - 1} - \frac z2
= \frac{z\,\eu^z}{\eu^z - 1} - \frac z2
= z + \frac{z}{\eu^z - 1} - \frac z2 = F(z) :
\]
$F$ is even, so $B_{2k+1} = 0$ for $k \geq 1$ (the lone odd
coefficient $B_1$ was absorbed by $+\frac z2$). Forward
pointer: $\cot w = \iu + \frac{2\iu}{\eu^{2\iu w}-1}$ gives
$w\cot w = 1 + \sum_{k\geq1}\frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!}(2\iu
w)^{2k}$, so the Laurent coefficients of the cotangent ---
hence, by \cref{ch:b3:residues}, every $\zeta(2k)$ --- are
priced by Bernoulli numbers:
\[
\zeta(2k) = (-1)^{k+1}\,\frac{(2\pi)^{2k}B_{2k}}{2\,(2k)!} .
\]

\textbf{21.} Write $f = \sum_nc_nz^n$ (radius $\infty$);
then $g(z) = \overline{f(\bar z)} = \sum_n\bar c_nz^n$ is
entire. On $\R$: $g(x) = \overline{f(x)} = f(x)$, so $g$ and
$f$ agree on a set with accumulation points in the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}
$\C$: \cref{thm:b3:holomorphic:identity} gives $g \equiv f$,
that is $f(\bar z) = \overline{f(z)}$ (equivalently: all
$c_n$ are real). For a real polynomial $P$:
$P(\bar\alpha) = \overline{P(\alpha)} = 0$, and the same
identity applied to the real derivatives $P', P'', \dots$
preserves multiplicities: nonreal roots pair up ---
question 8's pairing, reproved analytically.

\textbf{22.} The ladder, assembled: bounded $\Rightarrow$
constant (11); dominated by $A + B\abs z^m$ $\Rightarrow$
polynomial (12); real part bounded above $\Rightarrow$
constant (13); doubly periodic $\Rightarrow$ constant (14);
range missing a disc $\Rightarrow$ constant (15); proper
$\Rightarrow$ polynomial (16). Every rung is the Cauchy
formula: the value at a point is a circle average, so all
Taylor coefficients are priced by the size of $f$ on large
circles, and a growth cap annihilates coefficients
wholesale. Nothing of the sort constrains real $\mathcal
C^\infty$ functions: a \omterm{def:b3:lp:mollifier}{bump function}
(\cref{thm:b3:lp:regularization}) is bounded, compactly
supported and wildly nonconstant, and its derivatives at any
point outside the support all vanish without the function
vanishing anywhere near. Smoothness couples the derivatives
at distinct points not at all; \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphy} chains every
derivative to a single integral over a distant circle. A
local condition with a global informer --- that is why
entire functions obey law and order.

\textbf{23.} Expanding
$\abs{P(\eu^{\iu\theta})}^2 = \sum_{k,l}a_k\bar a_l
\eu^{\iu(k-l)\theta}$ and averaging kills every term $k \neq
l$: the mean is $\sum_k\abs{a_k}^2 =: N$. Suppose first that
$P$ has no zero on the unit circle, so $\theta \mapsto
\log\abs{P(\eu^{\iu\theta})}$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}. The bound $\log
t \leq t - 1$ applied to $t = \abs P^2/N$ gives, after
averaging,
\[
\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\log\abs{P(\eu^{\iu\theta})}^2
\dd\theta - \log N
\;\leq\; \frac1N\cdot N - 1 = 0,
\]
so the geometric mean of $\abs P$ is at most $\sqrt N$;
question 19 identifies that geometric mean as
$\abs c\prod_i\max(1, \abs{\alpha_i})$: Landau's
inequality. Roots on the circle: pick $r > 1$ distinct from
every $\abs{\alpha_i}$; the polynomial $P(rX)$, with roots
$\alpha_i/r$ off the unit circle and coefficients $a_kr^k$,
satisfies the inequality; both sides are \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} in $r$,
and letting $r \to 1^+$ gives the general case. On $X^2 -
X$: roots $0$ and $1$, so the left side is $1$, and the
right side is $\sqrt{1 + 1} = \sqrt2$: true, with room.

\textbf{24.} Write $\eu^z - 1 = z\,g(z)$ with $g(z) =
\sum_{k\geq0}\frac{z^k}{(k+1)!}$ entire, $g(0) = 1$. Since
$\eu^z = 1$ exactly on $2\pi\iu\Z$, $g$ has no zero in
$D(0, 2\pi)$ (for $0 < \abs z < 2\pi$ because $\eu^z - 1
\neq 0$, at $0$ by $g(0) = 1$), so $h = 1/g$ is \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic}
on $D(0,2\pi)$ and its Taylor series at $0$ --- by
definition $\sum\frac{B_n}{n!}z^n$ --- converges on the
whole disc: $\rho \geq 2\pi$. If $\rho > 2\pi$, the sum $S$
would be \omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on $D(0,\rho)$, and it agrees with $z
\mapsto z/(\eu^z - 1)$ on $0 < \abs z < 2\pi$; both are
\omterm{def:b3:holomorphic:holo}{holomorphic} on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{open set} $D(0,\rho) \setminus
2\pi\iu\Z$, so by the identity theorem they agree there. But
as $z \to 2\pi\iu$, $\abs{z/(\eu^z - 1)} \to \infty$
(numerator $\to 2\pi$, denominator $\to 0$) while $S$ is
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} at $2\pi\iu$: contradiction. Hence $\rho = 2\pi$
exactly, and Hadamard's formula gives
$\limsup_n\abs{B_n/n!}^{1/n} = \frac1{2\pi}$; the odd
coefficients being zero from $n = 3$ on, the $\limsup$ is
carried by the even indices, which is the stated formula
with $n = 2k$. Numerically at $k = 6$: $(2\pi)^{12} \approx
3.7858\cdot10^9$ and $2\cdot12! = 958\,003\,200$, so
$2\,(2k)!/(2\pi)^{2k} \approx 0.25305$, against
$\abs{B_{12}} = \frac{691}{2730} \approx 0.25311$. The
ratio, $1.00025$, is exactly $\zeta(12)$ to the digits
shown: the residue-calculus formula $\zeta(2k) =
(-1)^{k+1}\frac{(2\pi)^{2k}B_{2k}}{2\,(2k)!}$ of
\cref{ch:b3:residues} explains both the factor $2$ and the
tiny excess.

\textbf{25.} Cauchy bound: if $\abs z > 1 + M$ with $M =
\max_k\abs{q_k}$, then
\[
\Bigl|\sum_{k<n}q_kz^k\Bigr|
\leq M\,\frac{\abs z^n - 1}{\abs z - 1}
< \frac{M}{\abs z - 1}\,\abs z^n \leq \abs z^n,
\]
so $\abs{Q(z)} > 0$: all roots lie in $\overline D(0, 1+M)$.
The coefficients of the $P_j$ converge, hence are bounded by
some $M$: all roots of all $P_j$ (and of $P$) lie in the
\omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} $K = \overline D(0, 1 + M)$. Let $v_j \in K^n$ be a
vector listing the roots of $P_j$ with multiplicity
(question 7). Every subsequence of $(v_j)$ has a further
subsequence converging to some $(\beta_1, \dots, \beta_n)$;
the coefficients of $\prod_i(X - \alpha_i^{(j)})$ are, up to
sign, the elementary symmetric functions of $v_j$ ---
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} --- so along that subsequence they converge to
the coefficients of $\prod_i(X - \beta_i)$; but they
converge to those of $P$ by hypothesis, so $\prod_i(X -
\beta_i) = P$: every subsequential limit of $(v_j)$ is a
permutation of the root vector of $P$. If the matching
distance $\delta_j = \min_\sigma\max_i\,
\abs{\alpha_i^{(j)} - \alpha_{\sigma(i)}}$ did not tend to
$0$, a subsequence would keep $\delta_j \geq \varepsilon$
while its root vectors converge to a permutation of the
roots of $P$ --- forcing $\delta_j \to 0$ along it:
contradiction. So the root multisets converge. Sharpness:
$P_\varepsilon = (X - 1)^2 + \varepsilon$ has roots $1 \pm
\iu\sqrt\varepsilon$: the double root moves by
$\sqrt\varepsilon$, e.g.\ by $10^{-2}$ for $\varepsilon =
10^{-4}$. In general, if $\alpha$ is an $m$-fold root, then
near $\alpha$ one has $\abs{P(z)} \asymp \abs{z -
\alpha}^m$, so a size-$\varepsilon$ perturbation displaces
the cluster of roots by about $\varepsilon^{1/m}$: H\"older
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} of exponent $\frac1m$ and no better --- which is
why a numerical solver near a double root retains only half
the working digits.
