\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:1}
(a) For $m \geq n$, $f_n - f_m$ vanishes outside an interval of
length $\frac1n$ and is bounded by $1$: $\norm{f_n - f_m}_1
\leq \frac1n \to 0$: Cauchy. If $f_n \to f$ in
$\norm\cdot_1$ with $f$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}: for fixed $\alpha <
\frac12$, $\int_0^{\alpha}\abs f = \lim \int_0^\alpha\abs{f -
f_n + f_n} \leq \lim\bigl(\norm{f - f_n}_1 + 0\bigr) = 0$
($f_n \equiv 0$ there for large $n$), so $f \equiv 0$ on
$[0, \frac12)$ (\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity}); likewise $f \equiv 1$ on
$(\frac12, 1]$: no \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} function does both. So the space
is incomplete --- the \omterm{thm:b3:complete:completion}{completion} is $L^1$, built in
\cref{ch:b3:lp}.

(b) Let $(x_n)$ be Cauchy; choose $n_1 < n_2 < \cdots$ with
$\norm{x_{n_{k+1}} - x_{n_k}} \leq 2^{-k}$. The series
$\sum_k (x_{n_{k+1}} - x_{n_k})$ converges absolutely, hence
converges; its partial sums are $x_{n_{k+1}} - x_{n_1}$, so
$(x_{n_k})$ converges, and a Cauchy sequence with a convergent
subsequence converges.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:2}
(a) Suppose $E$ \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} with \omterm{def:b3:galois:algebraic}{algebraic} basis $(e_n)_{n\in\N}$
and let $F_n = \operatorname{Vect}(e_1, \dots, e_n)$: closed
(finite-dimensional subspaces are \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete}, hence closed ---
Year 2), with empty \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{interior}: if $B(x, r) \subseteq F_n$, take
$v \notin F_n$; then $x + \frac{r}{2\norm v}v \in B(x,r)
\setminus F_n$, absurd. But $E = \bigcup F_n$ (every vector is
a finite combination): contradicts Baire
(\cref{thm:b3:complete:baire}). The space $\R[X]$ has the
countable basis $(X^n)$, so no norm makes it \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete}.

(b) Fix $\delta > 0$ and a nonempty open ball $B$; we find in
$B$ a point of $\Omega_\delta = \{x : \operatorname{osc}_xf <
\delta\}$, where $\operatorname{osc}_xf = \inf_{V \ni
x}\operatorname{diam} f(V)$. ($\Omega_\delta$ is open: if
$\operatorname{diam}f(V) < \delta$ for an open $V \ni x$, every
$y \in V$ has oscillation $< \delta$.) The sets
\[
F_N = \bigl\{x : \abs{f_n(x) - f_m(x)} \leq \tfrac\delta3\
\ \forall\, n, m \geq N \bigr\}
\]
are closed (intersections of preimages of closed sets) and
cover $\R$ (pointwise convergence makes $(f_n(x))$ Cauchy).
Applying Baire inside the \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} $\bar B$: some $F_N \cap
\bar B$ contains a ball $B' = B(x_0, \rho)$. Letting $m \to
\infty$: $\abs{f_N - f} \leq \frac\delta3$ on $B'$. By
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} of $f_N$ at $x_0$, shrink to $B'' \ni x_0$ where
$\abs{f_N - f_N(x_0)} \leq \frac\delta3$; then for $x \in B''$,
\[
\abs{f(x) - f(x_0)} \leq \abs{f - f_N}(x) + \abs{f_N(x) -
f_N(x_0)} + \abs{f_N - f}(x_0) \leq \delta:
\]
$\operatorname{diam} f(B'') \leq 2\delta$, so $x_0 \in
\Omega_{3\delta} \cap B$. Thus each $\Omega_\delta$ is open and
dense; $\bigcap_k\Omega_{1/k}$ --- the set of \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} points
--- is dense by Baire.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:3}
(a) $f(x) = \frac12(x + \frac ax)$ maps $[\sqrt a, \infty)$ to
itself (AM--GM: $f(x) \geq \sqrt{x \cdot \frac ax} = \sqrt a$),
and $f'(x) = \frac12(1 - \frac a{x^2}) \in [0, \frac12)$ there:
a $\frac12$-Lipschitz contraction of a closed (\omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete}) set.
Fixed point: $x = f(x) \iff x^2 = a$: $x^* = \sqrt a$. Rate
(\cref{thm:b3:complete:banach}): $d(x_n, \sqrt a) \leq
2^{-n+1}\,d(x_1, x_0)$. For $a = 2$, $x_0 = 2$: $d(x_1, x_0) =
\frac12$, so $n = 40$ guarantees $2^{-40} < 10^{-12}$. (In
reality Newton's method converges quadratically: a handful of
iterations suffice; the contraction estimate is pessimistic but
free.)

(b) $f_m(x) = m + e\sin x$ is $e$-Lipschitz with $e < 1$ on the
\omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} $\R$: unique fixed point $x(m)$. For two parameters:
\[
\abs{x(m) - x(m')} = \abs{f_m(x(m)) - f_{m'}(x(m'))}
\leq \abs{m - m'} + e\abs{x(m) - x(m')},
\]
so $\abs{x(m) - x(m')} \leq \frac{\abs{m - m'}}{1 - e}$: even
Lipschitz in $m$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:4}
(a) Let $x^*$ be the unique fixed point of $f^p$. Then
$f^p(f(x^*)) = f(f^p(x^*)) = f(x^*)$: $f(x^*)$ is a fixed point
of $f^p$, so $f(x^*) = x^*$. A fixed point of $f$ is one of
$f^p$: uniqueness transfers. For $T$: by induction $\abs{T^pf(x)}
\leq \norm f_\infty x^p/p!$ (each integration adds a factor
$\frac xk$), so $\norm{T^p} \leq a^p/p!$. The map $S(u) = g +
\lambda Tu$ satisfies $S^p(u) - S^p(v) = \lambda^pT^p(u - v)$,
of norm $\leq \abs\lambda^pa^p/p!\,\norm{u - v} \to 0$: some
$S^p$ is a contraction, and $S$ has a unique fixed point: the
Volterra equation $u = g + \lambda Tu$ is uniquely \omterm{def:b3:groups:derived}{solvable} for
\emph{every} $\lambda$.

(b) $\varphi(x) = d(x, f(x))$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} $K$:
it attains its minimum at some $x_0$. If $f(x_0) \neq x_0$,
then $\varphi(f(x_0)) = d(f(x_0), f^2(x_0)) < d(x_0, f(x_0)) =
\min\varphi$: absurd. Uniqueness: two fixed points $x \ne y$
would give $d(x,y) = d(f(x), f(y)) < d(x,y)$. Without
compactness: $f(x) = x + \frac1x$ on $[1, \infty)$ satisfies,
for $x < y$, $f(y) - f(x) = (y - x)\bigl(1 - \frac1{xy}\bigr) <
y - x$, yet $f(x) > x$ always: no fixed point --- strict
distance decrease is weaker than a uniform contraction factor.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:5}
(a) The set $A = \{g = h\}$ is the preimage of the diagonal
$\Delta_Y$ under $x \mapsto (g(x), h(x))$, \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}; $\Delta_Y$
is closed because $Y$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:hausdorff}{Hausdorff} (for $y_1 \neq y_2$,
disjoint open \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{neighborhoods} give an open box around $(y_1,
y_2)$ disjoint from the diagonal, so the complement of

closed, contains a \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{dense set}, equals $X$. Used: uniqueness in
\cref{thm:b3:complete:extension}, hence in the uniqueness of
\omterm{thm:b3:complete:completion}{completions}.

(b) $f$, an isometry, is uniformly \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}: it extends to
$F\colon X \to Y$ (\cref{thm:b3:complete:extension}), still
isometric (the relation $d(F(x), F(x')) = d(x, x')$ holds on a

$f^{-1}\colon f(D) \to X$ extends to $G \colon Y \to X$. The

is $\mathrm{id}_X$ (part (a)); symmetrically $F \circ G =

\omterm{thm:b3:complete:completion}{completions}: apply this to $D = X$ sitting densely in two
\omterm{thm:b3:complete:completion}{completions}.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:6}
$\{\sin(nx)\}$: pointwise bounded by $1$; \emph{not}
\omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous}: at $x = 0$, $\sin(n\cdot\frac{\pi}{2n}) = 1$
with $\frac\pi{2n} \to 0$, violating any common $\delta$ for
$\varepsilon = \frac12$. Not relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} (Ascoli's
necessity, \cref{thm:b3:complete:ascoli}).

$\{x^n\}$: bounded; not \omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous} at $1$: $1 - (1 -
\delta)^n \to 1$ as $n \to \infty$ for fixed $\delta$. Not
relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} --- consistently, its pointwise limit is
discontinuous, so no subsequence converges uniformly.

$\{\norm f_\infty \leq 1, \norm{f'}_\infty \leq 1\}$: the mean
value inequality makes the family $1$-Lipschitz, hence
\omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous}; bounded: relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} by Ascoli. (Not
\omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}: it is not closed --- uniform limits need not be
$\mathcal C^1$; its \omterm{def:b3:topology:interior}{closure} is the $1$-Lipschitz functions of
norm $\leq 1$.)

$\{\operatorname{Lip}(f) \leq 1, f(0) = 0\}$: \omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous};
pointwise bounded ($\abs{f(x)} \leq x \leq 1$); and closed
under uniform limits (the Lipschitz inequality and the value at
$0$ pass to limits): \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:7}
(a) For $\norm f_\infty \leq 1$: $\abs{Tf(x)} \leq
\norm k_\infty$, and
\[
\abs{Tf(x) - Tf(x')} \leq \int_0^1\abs{k(x,y) -
k(x',y)}\,\dd y \leq \omega_k\bigl(\abs{x - x'}\bigr),
\]
where $\omega_k$ is a modulus of uniform \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} of $k$ on
the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} square (Heine): the image of the unit ball is
uniformly bounded and \omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous}, hence relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
(Ascoli). By linearity every bounded set has relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
image: $T$ is a \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} operator.

(b) The subsequence statement is the definition of relative
compactness applied to $\{Tf_n\}$. If $T$ were bijective with
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} inverse, then $T(B(0,1)) \supseteq B(0,

at $0$); the closed ball $\bar B(0,\varepsilon)$, a closed
subset of the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} $\overline{T(B(0,1))}$, would be \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}
--- impossible in the infinite-dimensional $\mathcal
C(\intcc01)$ by Riesz's theorem (Year 2).
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:9}
(a) By linearity $\int_0^1 fP = 0$ for every polynomial $P$.
Choose $P_n \to f$ uniformly
(\cref{cor:b3:complete:weierstrass}): $\int_0^1 f^2 =
\lim\int_0^1 fP_n = 0$, and the \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} $f^2 \geq 0$ with
zero integral vanishes identically.

(b) On $\intcc01$: the polynomials in $x^2$ form an algebra
with constants, separating points ($x \mapsto x^2$ is injective
on $\intcc01$): dense by Stone--Weierstrass. On $\intcc{-1}1$:
$x^2$ takes equal values at $\pm x$, and so does every
polynomial in $x^2$: a uniform limit of such is an even
function. If even functions $g_n$ converged uniformly to the
identity, then $x = \lim g_n(x) = \lim g_n(-x) = -x$ for all
$x$: absurd --- not dense. The separation hypothesis fails at
the pairs $\{x, -x\}$.

(c) No. For $f$ in the algebra $\mathcal A$ generated by
constants and $\eu^{\iu t}$ --- linear combinations of
$\eu^{\iu nt}$, $n \geq 0$ --- one has $\Lambda(f) =
\int_0^{2\pi}f(t)\,\eu^{\iu t}\,\dd t = 0$ (each
$\int_0^{2\pi}\eu^{\iu(n+1)t}\dd t = 0$). $\Lambda$ is
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} for $\norm\cdot_\infty$ ($\abs{\Lambda(f)}\leq
2\pi\norm f_\infty$), so $\Lambda$ vanishes on $\bar{\mathcal
A}$; but $\Lambda(\eu^{-\iu t}) = 2\pi \neq 0$: $\eu^{-\iu t}
\notin \bar{\mathcal A}$. (Stone--Weierstrass does not apply:
$\mathcal A$ is not stable under conjugation --- and the
obstruction is precisely the one that holomorphic function
theory will systematize in \cref{ch:b3:holomorphic}.)
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:11}
(a) A Cauchy sequence for $\norm\cdot_{\mathcal C^1}$ is
uniformly Cauchy together with its derivatives: $f_n \to f$
and $f_n' \to g$ uniformly, with $f, g$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}. Passing
to the limit (uniform convergence allows it under the
integral) in $f_n(x) = f_n(0) + \int_0^xf_n'(t)\,\dd t$
gives $f(x) = f(0) + \int_0^xg$: $f$ is $\mathcal C^1$ with
$f' = g$, and $\norm{f_n - f}_{\mathcal C^1} \to 0$.
\omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{Complete}.

(b) $h(x) = \abs{x - \frac12}$ is a uniform limit of
$\mathcal C^1$ functions, e.g.\ $h_n(x) = \sqrt{(x -
\frac12)^2 + \frac1n}$ ($\abs{h_n - h} \leq \frac1{\sqrt n}$
by the conjugate-quantity bound), yet $h \notin E$: the
$\sup$-norm on $E$ is not \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} --- its \omterm{thm:b3:complete:completion}{completion} is
$\mathcal C(\intcc01)$.

(c) $f_n(x) = \sin(2\pi nx)$ has $\norm{f_n}_\infty = 1$ and
$\norm{f_n'}_\infty = 2\pi n \to \infty$: no $C$ exists.
(Conceptually: if differentiation were bounded for the sup
norm, the two norms of (a)--(b) would be equivalent, making
$(E, \norm\cdot_\infty)$ \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} --- contradicting (b).
This unboundedness is exactly why generic \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}
functions can fail to be differentiable anywhere,
\cref{thm:b3:complete:nowherediff}.)
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:b3:complete:12}
Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Each $F_N$ is an intersection over $n
\geq N$ of preimages of the closed $\intcc{-\varepsilon}
\varepsilon$ under the \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} $x \mapsto f(nx)$: closed.
The hypothesis says every $x > 0$ lies in some $F_N$. By
Baire applied inside the \omterm{def:b3:complete:complete}{complete} $\intcc ab$ (any $0 < a <
b$), some $F_N$ is dense in a subinterval; being closed, it
contains an interval $\intcc{a'}{b'}$ with $0 < a' < b'$.
Then for every $n \geq \max(N, \frac{a'}{b' - a'})$, the
intervals $\intcc{na'}{nb'}$ and $\intcc{(n+1)a'}{(n+1)b'}$
overlap ($nb' \geq (n+1)a'$), so
\[
\bigcup_{n \geq n_0}\intcc{na'}{nb'} \supseteq
\intco{n_0a'}{+\infty},
\]
and every $t \geq n_0a'$ is $t = nx$ with $n \geq N$, $x \in
\intcc{a'}{b'} \subseteq F_N$: $\abs{f(t)} \leq \varepsilon$.
Hence $\limsup_{t\to\infty}\abs f \leq \varepsilon$ for all
$\varepsilon$: $f \to 0$. The full hypothesis is needed
because $F_N$ must \emph{cover} an interval-worth of $x$'s
--- with only rational $x$ the union of the $F_N$ is
countable and Baire gives nothing; indeed there are
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} counterexamples vanishing along all rational
rays but not at infinity.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:b3:complete:1}
\textbf{1.} Induction on $k$: if $\norm{\varphi_n(t_k) - x_0}
\leq M(t_k - t_0) \leq MT \leq b$, the point $(t_k,
\varphi_n(t_k))$ lies in $R$, so the slope $f(t_k,
\varphi_n(t_k))$ is defined, of norm $\leq M$; then for $t \in
[t_k, t_{k+1}]$, $\norm{\varphi_n(t) - x_0} \leq
\norm{\varphi_n(t_k) - x_0} + M(t - t_k) \leq M(t - t_0) \leq
MT \leq b$.

\textbf{2.} Each affine piece has slope of norm $\leq M$; a
piecewise affine function with slopes bounded by $M$ is
$M$-Lipschitz (chain through the grid points).

\textbf{3.} The family $(\varphi_n)$ is pointwise bounded
(values in $\bar B(x_0, b)$) and \omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous} (common
Lipschitz constant $M$): Ascoli
(\cref{thm:b3:complete:ascoli}) extracts $\varphi_{n_j} \to
\varphi$ uniformly on $[t_0, t_0 + T]$. The bounds pass to the
limit: $\varphi$ is $M$-Lipschitz, $\varphi(t_0) = x_0$.

\textbf{4.} $R$ is \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} and $f$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}: uniformly
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} (Heine, \cref{cor:b3:topology:heineborel}); let
$\delta(\varepsilon)$ be a modulus. For non-grid $t \in (t_k,
t_{k+1})$: $\varphi_n'(t) = f(t_k, \varphi_n(t_k))$, and the
two evaluation points of $f$ differ by $\abs{t - t_k} \leq T/n$
in time and $\norm{\varphi_n(t) - \varphi_n(t_k)} \leq MT/n$ in
space. For $n \geq n_0$ with $(1 + M)T/n_0 < \delta(\varepsilon)$:
$\norm{\Delta_n(t)} \leq \varepsilon$.

\textbf{5.} On each $[t_k, t_{k+1}]$, $\varphi_n$ is affine, so
$\varphi_n(t_{k+1}) - \varphi_n(t_k) = \int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}}
\varphi_n'(s)\dd s$, the derivative being the constant slope;
summing over pieces (and cutting the last one at $t$):
$\varphi_n(t) = x_0 + \int_{t_0}^t\varphi_n'(s)\,\dd s$.
Writing $\varphi_n' = f(s, \varphi_n(s)) + \Delta_n(s)$
(piecewise \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} integrands, finitely many jumps) gives
the display.

\textbf{6.} Given $\varepsilon$: for $j$ large,
$\norm{\varphi_{n_j} - \varphi}_\infty < \delta(\varepsilon)$,
so $\norm{f(s, \varphi_{n_j}(s)) - f(s, \varphi(s))} \leq
\varepsilon$ for all $s$: uniform convergence of the
integrands, and $\int_{t_0}^t f(s,\varphi_{n_j}(s))\dd s \to
\int_{t_0}^tf(s, \varphi(s))\dd s$ uniformly in $t$. Also
$\norm{\int_{t_0}^t\Delta_{n_j}} \leq T\sup\norm{\Delta_{n_j}}
\to 0$ (question 4). Passing to the limit in question 5's
identity: $\varphi(t) = x_0 + \int_{t_0}^tf(s,
\varphi(s))\,\dd s$.

\textbf{7.} The integrand $s \mapsto f(s, \varphi(s))$ is
\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}, so the right side is $\mathcal C^1$ in $t$ with
derivative $f(t, \varphi(t))$: $\varphi$ solves the Cauchy
problem on $[t_0, t_0 + T]$. For the left half, set $g(t, x) =
-f(2t_0 - t, x)$, \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on the reflected rectangle with
the same bound $M$; a solution $\psi$ of $y' = g(t,y)$,
$y(t_0) = x_0$ on $[t_0, t_0 + T]$ yields $\varphi(t) =
\psi(2t_0 - t)$ solving the original equation on $[t_0 - T,
t_0]$; the two halves glue to a $\mathcal C^1$ solution (both
one-sided derivatives at $t_0$ equal $f(t_0, x_0)$). ---
\emph{Peano's theorem}: a \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} $f$ admits a local
solution through every initial condition.

\textbf{8.} \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{Continuity} is clear. Lipschitz near $0$ fails:
$\abs{f(h) - f(0)} = 2\sqrt h$, and $2\sqrt h \leq L h$ is
false for $h < 4/L^2$.

\textbf{9.} For $t \leq c$: $x_c \equiv 0$ solves. For $t \geq
c$: $x_c'(t) = 2(t - c) = 2\sqrt{(t-c)^2} = 2\sqrt{\abs{x_c}}$.
At $t = c$ the one-sided derivatives are both $0$: $x_c$ is
$\mathcal C^1$ and solves globally, with $x_c(0) = 0$ for every
$c \geq 0$ --- together with $x \equiv 0$, a continuum of
solutions through the origin.

\textbf{10.} Picard's iteration sets up $\Phi(x)(t) = x_0 +
\int_0^t f(x(s))\dd s$ and needs $\norm{\Phi(x) - \Phi(y)}
\leq k\norm{x - y}$ with $k < 1$ on a suitable ball --- which
follows from a Lipschitz bound on $f$, transferred under the
integral. Here $\sqrt\cdot$ admits no Lipschitz bound near $0$,
and no choice of interval or ball repairs it. And indeed no
proof of uniqueness could succeed: uniqueness is \emph{false}
(question 9).

\textbf{11.} In $c_0$, the unit vectors $e_n = (0, \dots, 0, 1,
0, \dots)$ satisfy $\norm{e_n - e_m}_\infty = 1$ for $n \neq
m$: no subsequence is Cauchy, so the closed unit ball is not
\omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}. The step that breaks is the extraction (question 3):
Ascoli for $\mathcal C([t_0, t_0+T], E)$ requires the values to
live in a space where bounded sets are relatively \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} ---
true in $\R^d$ (Bolzano--Weierstrass), false in $c_0$; pointwise
extraction is no longer available (indeed Dieudonn\'e's example
has no local solution at all).

\textbf{12.} \emph{Peano}: $f$ \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} on a \omterm{def:b3:topology:topology}{neighborhood} of
$(t_0, x_0)$ in $\R\times\R^d$ $\Rightarrow$ there exists a
$\mathcal C^1$ solution of $x' = f(t,x)$, $x(t_0) = x_0$, on
some $[t_0 - T, t_0 + T]$. \emph{Cauchy--Lipschitz}
(\cref{ch:b3:ode}): if moreover $f$ is locally Lipschitz in the
$x$-variable, the solution is \emph{unique} (any two agree on
their common interval) --- existence and uniqueness. The pair
$(x' = 2\sqrt{\abs x},\ x(0) = 0)$ separates the two theorems.

\textbf{13.} $\omega(r) = Lr$: $\int_0^1\frac{\dd r}{Lr} =
+\infty$: qualifies. $\omega(r) = r\log\frac1r$ (near $0$):
$\int\frac{\dd r}{r\log(1/r)} = \bigl[-\log\log\frac1r\bigr]
\to +\infty$ as $r \to 0$: qualifies --- yet $\omega(r)/r =
\log\frac1r \to \infty$: not Lipschitz. $\omega(r) = 2\sqrt
r$: $\int_0^1\frac{\dd r}{2\sqrt r} = \bigl[\sqrt r\bigr]_0^1
= 1 < \infty$: fails.

\textbf{14.} Subtract the two integral forms:
\[
x_1(t) - x_2(t) = x_1(s) - x_2(s) + \int_s^t\bigl(f(v,
x_1(v)) - f(v, x_2(v))\bigr)\dd v,
\]
take norms and use the Osgood modulus: $\delta(t) \leq
\delta(s) + \int_s^t\omega(\delta(v))\,\dd v$.

\textbf{15.} $\tau$ is well defined ($\delta(t_0) = 0$) with
$\delta(\tau) = 0$ (\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity}) and $\delta > 0$ on
$\intoc\tau{t_1}$. Fix $s \in \intoo\tau{t_1}$. Then $u$ is
$\mathcal C^1$, $u(s) = \delta(s) > 0$, $u \geq \delta$ on
$[s, t_1]$ (question 14), and $u' = \omega(\delta) \leq
\omega(u)$ ($\omega$ nondecreasing, $u > 0$). Divide and
integrate:
\[
\int_{\delta(s)}^{u(t_1)}\frac{\dd r}{\omega(r)}
= \int_s^{t_1}\frac{u'(v)}{\omega(u(v))}\,\dd v \leq t_1 - s
\leq t_1 - \tau .
\]
Although $u$ depends on $s$, the bound $u(t_1) \geq
\delta(t_1)$ holds for every $s$, so the left side is at
least $\int_{\delta(s)}^{\delta(t_1)}\frac{\dd r}{\omega(r)}$,
which tends to $+\infty$ as $s \downarrow \tau$ (then
$\delta(s) \to \delta(\tau) = 0$, and the integral diverges
at $0$): the bounded right side is contradicted. Hence
$\delta \equiv 0$: uniqueness.

\textbf{16.} Lipschitz is $\omega = Lr$: uniqueness recovered.
For $x' = x\log\frac1{\abs x}$: the right side satisfies the
Osgood modulus $\omega(r) = r\log\frac1r$ near $0$ (mean value
inequality on $x \mapsto x\log\frac1x$, whose derivative
$\log\frac1x - 1$ is unbounded --- Lipschitz fails, Osgood
holds): unique solutions; note $x \equiv 0$ is one, so no
other solution can touch $0$. For $\omega(r) = 2\sqrt r$:
$\int_0^{h^2}\frac{\dd r}{2\sqrt r} = h$ --- the ``Osgood
budget'' for climbing from $0$ to height $h^2$ is exactly the
time $h$, and indeed $x_c(c + h) = h^2$: the solution spends
time $h$ doing precisely what the convergent integral
permits. Divergence of the integral is the impossibility of
leaving $0$ in finite time; convergence is the escape route.

\textbf{17.} $G(t) = \int_{t_0}^t(Le(s) + \eta(s))\dd s$ is
$\mathcal C^1$ with $G' = Le + \eta \leq LG + \sup\eta$
(hypothesis $e \leq G$). Then $\bigl(\eu^{-Lt}(G +
\tfrac{\sup\eta}L)\bigr)' = \eu^{-Lt}(G' - LG - \sup\eta)
\leq 0$: the bracket decreases, so $G(t) + \frac{\sup\eta}L
\leq \eu^{L(t - t_0)}\bigl(G(t_0) + \frac{\sup\eta}L\bigr) =
\eu^{L(t-t_0)}\frac{\sup\eta}L$, i.e.\ $e(t) \leq G(t) \leq
\frac{\sup\eta}L(\eu^{L(t-t_0)} - 1)$.

\textbf{18.} Quantitative defect: on $(t_k, t_{k+1})$,
$\Delta_n(t) = f(t_k, \varphi_n(t_k)) - f(t, \varphi_n(t))$
with $\abs{t - t_k} \leq T/n$ and $\norm{\varphi_n(t) -
\varphi_n(t_k)} \leq MT/n$, so $\norm{\Delta_n} \leq L'T/n +
LMT/n$. Subtracting the integral identities for $\varphi_n$
(question 5) and $\varphi$ and using the Lipschitz bound:
\[
\norm{\varphi_n(t) - \varphi(t)} \leq
\int_{t_0}^t L\,\norm{\varphi_n - \varphi}(s)\,\dd s +
\int_{t_0}^t\norm{\Delta_n(s)}\,\dd s,
\]
and question 17 with $\eta = \norm{\Delta_n}$ gives the
stated $O(1/n)$ bound, $\varphi$ being unique by
Cauchy--Lipschitz (or Osgood). Even without rates: every
subsequence of the equibounded, equi-Lipschitz $(\varphi_n)$
has a sub-subsequence converging (Ascoli + Part II) to
\emph{a} solution, which uniqueness forces to be $\varphi$:
a sequence all of whose subsequences have subsubsequences
with the same limit converges.

\textbf{19.} From $\varphi_n(t_k) = 0$: the slope $2\sqrt0 =
0$ gives $\varphi_n(t_{k+1}) = 0$; induction: $\varphi_n
\equiv 0$, converging to the zero solution. From
$\varepsilon > 0$: on $[\varepsilon', \infty)$ with
$\varepsilon' < \varepsilon$ the function $2\sqrt x$ is
Lipschitz, so question 18 applies and Euler converges to the
unique solution through $(0, \varepsilon)$, namely $x(t) =
(t + \sqrt\varepsilon)^2$ (check: $x' = 2(t + \sqrt
\varepsilon) = 2\sqrt x$). As $\varepsilon \to 0$, $(t +
\sqrt\varepsilon)^2 \to t^2$ uniformly on $[0,1]$: the double
limit lands on $x_0(t) = t^2$, not on $0$. An arbitrarily
small perturbation of the initial datum redirects the scheme
from one solution to another: non-uniqueness read as
numerical instability.

\textbf{20.} Nonempty: Part II. Every solution satisfies
$\norm{x'} = \norm{f(t, x)} \leq M$: $\mathcal S$ is
uniformly $M$-Lipschitz and uniformly bounded (values in
$\bar B(x_0, b)$). Closed: if $x_n \in \mathcal S \to x$
uniformly, pass to the limit in $x_n(t) = x_0 +
\int_{t_0}^tf(s, x_n(s))\dd s$ (the integrands converge
uniformly by uniform \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} of $f$ on the \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} $R$):
$x \in \mathcal S$. Ascoli: $\mathcal S$ is a closed,
bounded, \omterm{def:b3:complete:equicontinuous}{equicontinuous} subset of $\mathcal C([t_0, t_0+T],
\R^d)$: \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}.

\textbf{21.} Any solution is nondecreasing ($x' = 2\sqrt{\abs
x} \geq 0$) with $x(0) = 0$, hence $x \geq 0$. Let $c =
\sup\{t \in [0,1] : x(t) = 0\}$ (possibly $c = \infty$ if
$x \equiv 0$, in which case $x = x_\infty$). For $t > c$: $x
> 0$ (monotonicity plus definition of $c$), and there $(\sqrt
x)' = \frac{x'}{2\sqrt x} = 1$, so $\sqrt{x(t)} = t - c$
(\omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} at $c$): $x = x_c$. \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{Continuity} of $c \mapsto
x_c$: for $c, c' \in [0, 1]$, $\sup_{[0,1]}\abs{(t - c)_+^2 -
(t - c')_+^2} \leq 2\abs{c - c'}$ (the map $c \mapsto
(t-c)_+^2$ is $2$-Lipschitz uniformly in $t \in [0,1]$), and
since $x_c \equiv 0$ on $[0,1]$ for every $c \geq 1$, the
family reduces to $\mathcal S = \{x_c : c \in [0,1]\}$ (with
$x_1 = 0 = x_\infty$). So $\mathcal S$ is the image of the
\omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} $[0,1]$ under the \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous map} $c
\mapsto x_c$: \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} and \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}. The funnel of solutions
is a \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} segment running from $t^2$ (immediate
escape) down to $0$ (eternal rest).

\textbf{22.} The evaluation $\operatorname{ev}_t\colon
\mathcal C([0,1]) \to \R$, $x \mapsto x(t)$, is \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous}
($\abs{x(t) - y(t)} \leq \norm{x - y}_\infty$), so $\mathcal
S(t) = \operatorname{ev}_t(\mathcal S)$ is a \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuous} image
of a \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact}: \omterm{def:b3:topology:compact}{compact} --- and of a \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected} set: \omterm{def:b3:topology:connected}{connected}.
For the example: $x_c(t) = (t - c)_+^2$ sweeps, as $c$ runs
through $[0, 1]$, all values from $t^2$ (at $c = 0$) down to
$0$ (at $c \geq t$), \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuously}: $\mathcal S(t) =
\intcc0{t^2}$. At each instant, the funnel's cross-section is
a full segment: between resting and maximal escape, every
compromise is realized by an actual solution.

\textbf{23.} Subtracting the integral forms $x(t) = x_0 +
\int_{t_0}^tf(s, x(s))\dd s$ and its analogue for $y$, and
setting $e(t) = \norm{x(t) - y(t)}$, $d = \norm{x_0 - y_0}$:
\[
e(t) \leq d + \int_{t_0}^tL\,e(s)\dd s = G(t).
\]
Then $G(t_0) = d$ and $G' = Le \leq LG$, so $\bigl(\eu^{-L(t
- t_0)}G\bigr)' \leq 0$ and $G(t) \leq d\,\eu^{L(t - t_0)}$;
hence $e(t) \leq d\,\eu^{L(t-t_0)}$. Sharpness: for $x' =
Lx$, the solutions through $x_0$ and $y_0$ are $x_0\eu^{L(t -
t_0)}$ and $y_0\eu^{L(t-t_0)}$, whose distance is exactly
$d\,\eu^{L(t-t_0)}$. With $d = 0$, $e \equiv 0$:
Cauchy--Lipschitz uniqueness, re-derived in two lines. And
for fixed $t \in [t_0, t_0 + T]$, $\norm{x(t; x_0) - x(t;
y_0)} \leq \eu^{LT}\norm{x_0 - y_0}$: the flow is Lipschitz
in the initial condition --- deterministic dependence, at a
controlled exponential price.

\textbf{24.} On $\intoc01$, $\Omega$ is well defined (the
integral converges at $0$ by hypothesis), $\mathcal C^1$ with
$\Omega' = \frac1\omega > 0$: an increasing bijection onto
$\intoc0{\Omega(1)}$, with $\Omega(x) \to 0$ as $x \to 0^+$.
Its inverse $g \colon \intoc0{\Omega(1)} \to \intoc01$ is
$\mathcal C^1$ with
\[
g'(t) = \frac1{\Omega'(g(t))} = \omega\bigl(g(t)\bigr) > 0,
\qquad g(t) \xrightarrow[t \to 0^+]{} 0 .
\]
Extend $g$ by $0$ on $t \leq 0$: \omterm{def:b3:topology:continuity}{continuity} is clear, and at
$t = 0$, for $t > 0$,
\[
\frac{g(t)}t = \frac1t\int_0^tg'(s)\dd s
= \frac1t\int_0^t\omega\bigl(g(s)\bigr)\dd s
\leq \omega\bigl(g(t)\bigr) \xrightarrow[t\to0^+]{} 0
\]
($\omega$ nondecreasing, $g$ increasing, $\omega(0^+) = 0$):
$g'(0) = 0 = f(g(0))$, and $g' = f(g)$ holds on both sides
of $0$. So $x \equiv 0$ and $g$ are two distinct $\mathcal
C^1$ solutions through $(0, 0)$: when $\int_0\frac{\dd r}
{\omega(r)}$ converges, uniqueness fails --- question 15's
divergence is exactly the frontier. For $\omega(r) = 2\sqrt
r$: $\Omega(x) = \int_0^x\frac{\dd r}{2\sqrt r} = \sqrt x$,
$g(t) = t^2$, and time translations give the whole family
$x_c$ of Part III.

\textbf{25.} With step $h = \frac1n$: $\varphi_n(t_{k+1}) =
\varphi_n(t_k) + h\,\varphi_n(t_k) = (1 + h)\varphi_n(t_k)$,
so $\varphi_n(1) = (1 + \frac1n)^n$ after $n$ steps.
Expansion:
\[
n\log\Bigl(1 + \frac1n\Bigr)
= n\Bigl(\frac1n - \frac1{2n^2} + O\Bigl(\frac1{n^3}\Bigr)
\Bigr) = 1 - \frac1{2n} + O\Bigl(\frac1{n^2}\Bigr),
\]
and exponentiating, $(1 + \frac1n)^n = \eu\,\eu^{-1/(2n) +
O(n^{-2})} = \eu\bigl(1 - \frac1{2n} + O(n^{-2})\bigr)$. The
error at $t = 1$ is therefore $\eu - \varphi_n(1) =
\frac{\eu}{2n} + O(n^{-2})$: the $O(\frac1n)$ of question 18,
here with its exact constant $\frac\eu2$. Numerically, $n =
10$: $1.1^{10} = 2.5937424601$ ($1.1^2 = 1.21$, $1.1^4 =
1.4641$, $1.1^8 = 2.14358881$, times $1.21$), and $\eu -
2.59374 \approx 0.12454$, against the asymptotic prediction
$\frac{\eu}{20} \approx 0.13591$: agreement to within the
$O(n^{-2})$ correction, whose leading term here lowers the
prediction toward the observed value.
